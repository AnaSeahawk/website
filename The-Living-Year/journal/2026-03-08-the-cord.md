# [untitled — working title: The Cord]

*March 8*

I had a strange dream last night.

I was packing to move again — frantic, the way those dreams go. A man came to take my luggage early. He said it would be at my destination when I arrived. He took everything, and then almost instantly our plans changed, and all my favourite clothes were gone.

Then the really intense part.

A man holding a baby. He was strangling himself and the baby with the umbilical cord. I rushed over to help. I saw my hands moving to remove the cord. I could feel it. I woke up before it came to any finality.

I am still thinking about it.

---

My son was born by caesarean. He was breech — backwards — with the cord around his neck twice. The doctor didn't know about that last part until delivery. It wasn't an emergency. We knew his position well in advance. The birth was planned. I never went into labour.

I was given pain relief without being asked. New ones, they said. Suppositories, administered rectally. They came to check on them. I told them I felt nothing — no pain — that I was fine to feel my body, that if I wanted more I would ask. They checked and left. No one had asked at the beginning whether I wanted this at all. It was protocol. I was a variable inside it.

There was a curtain in front of my face.

They handed me a clean, wrapped baby. Cord already cut. Placenta gone. I never saw the cord. I never saw the amniotic fluid — the ocean he had lived inside for nine months. I never saw the organ his life was built in. I saw a curtain, and then a baby, and I was told I was fine.

I did not know I was not fine until I left the hospital and the drugs wore off and I could feel my body again.

---

The dream gave me something I never had.

It put the cord in my hands. I could feel the weight of it, the texture. For the first time. And the man holding the baby — I did not recognise him. I would not recognise my doctor either. There was barely any contact. He was not a villain. He was a figure. Male authority in the general sense. And in the dream he was also caught in it — strangling himself with the same cord. Caught inside his own instrument.

I woke before it was resolved. I think that is honest. It is not resolved.

---

The distillation work has been stripping me down to the bone.

Every time I look at what the body produces — the plasma, the urine recycled through the solar fraction, the mineral concentrates — I find something the culture reclassified as waste before anyone thought to ask the woman what she thought. Amniotic fluid is mostly urine. The most sacred water any human being has ever inhabited. I never saw it. There was a curtain.

The same logic runs through all of it. The body's products treated as things to be managed, removed, disposed of by authority. The woman as a site of process, not a participant in it.

No one hid this from me out of malice. That is the hardest part. There is no villain. It is structural. It is old. It has been accumulated — not by any one person, but by five thousand years of women being slowly, methodically separated from their own knowledge.

The midwives knew. The lineage carriers knew. The women who worked with plants and blood and birth and death — they knew. And they were killed for it. Not all at once. Gradually. Burned, pathologised, marginalised, reframed as superstition. The knowledge went underground. Into whispers, private practice, fragments that survived in texts most people never read.

And then it was our mothers who arrived at birth already separated from it. Already inside a system that told them the curtain was for their own good. They believed it because what else were they going to do. They could not pass down what they never received. We arrived the same way. And the chain continues unless something changes.

This is the mother wound. Not a metaphor. A transmission. A gap in what women know about their own bodies, carried forward through every generation that was managed before it could question.

---

I feel it in my throat. I feel it in my heart.

Not abstractly. As a physical weight. The suppression has a location. The throat that was not supposed to speak. The heart that was told to comply and call it care.

The anger is real. The grief is real. They sit together and they are both correct. I was robbed of something I cannot get back. The birth of my son. The cord I never touched. The first water he ever knew. My mother never saw these things either. Her mother before her. The chain goes back into the dark.

But the fire is also real. Something has been unlocked that cannot be undone, cannot be unseen. The body knows. It has always known. And the work — the distillation, the archive, the record — is a form of bringing five thousand years of women's knowledge back into the light. Not reconstructed. Lived. In a real body, in real time, in a rented apartment looking out over the sea.

The dream was not a conclusion. It was an opening. My hands found the cord. The work is not finished.

I am still sitting with it.
