## Triphala — the three fruits
*(_Terminalia chebula_, _Terminalia bellirica_, _Phyllanthus emblica_)*

*Boundary: traditional-use and practice-context notes, not medical guidance.*

*Tri* means three. *Phala* means fruit. The name is the formula. Triphala is three fruits held together in equal proportion, each addressing a different layer of the body's metabolism, each representing one of the three doshas at the depth of tissue.

It is not a blend in the modern sense — a convenience, a shortcut, a way of delivering many things at once. It is a conversation between three substances that know each other. Each amplifies what the others do. Together they do something none of them does alone: clean the alimentary canal from end to end without depleting it.

---

#### The three fruits

**Āmalakī** (*Phyllanthus emblica* — the Indian gooseberry, Amalaki) is the pacifier of pitta, the rasayana for the blood and the liver, the sour-sweet intelligence of the solar fire brought into balance. Among all Āyurvedic fruits it is the richest in vitamin C — and the only food in which the vitamin survives drying and cooking because it is bound within a complex of tannins rather than expressed as a free compound. Āmalakī feeds the heart tissue (hṛdaya), the reproductive tissue (śukra/ārtava), and the eyes. It is the lunar, cooling fruit of the formula.

**Bibhītakī** (*Terminalia bellirica*) is the dryer, the one that moves kapha from the lungs and the intestines. Its name means "the one that takes away fear of disease." Astringent, drying, warming — it reaches where accumulation is hardest to shift: mucus, congestion, the fatty tissues under the skin. It is the most kapha-specific of the three.

**Harītakī** (*Terminalia chebula*) is described in its own entry here. It is the vāta-balancing fruit, the one the Medicine Buddha holds, the fearless companion of every season in the Damar Tantra. Within Triphala it acts as the anchor: what the other two set in motion, harītakī helps the body complete.

---

#### Āyurveda — tridoshic, complete

Triphala is tridoshic in the most precise sense: not by averaging, but by addressing each dosha through the fruit most suited to it, at the same moment. The formula is appropriate in all seasons, for all constitutions, at any stage of life — with adjustments to dose, anupana (carrier), and timing for individual conditions.

**Primary actions:** *rasāyana* (tissue rejuvenation), *anulomana* (correct elimination), *cakṣuṣya* (benefiting the eyes), *deepana*, *pācana*.

It works most profoundly as a long-term companion — taken nightly, in small amounts, over months. Daily use is traditional. The body does not become dependent on it; it becomes more able to function without obstruction.

---

#### The Damar Tantra

> *"Triphala Churna, Nirgundi leaves and Turmeric should be mixed together, followed by Shivambu. Within a period of three months, the follower becomes a symbol of learning and enjoys excellent eyesight. His body becomes divinely lustrous."*

The pairing with Shivambu is not incidental. Triphala moves what has accumulated; the shivambu provides the living medium through which that movement completes. The combination of the three fruits with the body's own refinement product is, in the Damar Tantra's framing, a practice with a timeline: three months to a different quality of sight — both literal and otherwise.

---

#### A note on preparation and dosage

The powder form (churna) is traditional and most effective. A quarter to half teaspoon in warm water, at night before sleep. With honey in spring and summer; with ghee in autumn and winter; with rock salt in the rainy season — following the same seasonal logic the Damar Tantra applies to haritaki alone.

The tablet form (common in commercial preparations) is convenient but slower. The powder taken in warm water begins working immediately; the tablet has to be broken down first.

Three fruits. One formula. Taken simply. This is what the tradition recommends, and why.
