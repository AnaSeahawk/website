## Nettle — feed me
*(_Urtica dioica_ L.)*

*Boundary: traditional-use and felt-sense notes, not medical guidance.*

Nettle is not a medicine in the protocol sense. It is food.

This distinction matters. Most of what fills the supplement aisle is directing the body — stimulating this, suppressing that, pushing a process in a particular direction. Nettle does not direct. It supplies. It is one of the most mineral-dense plants available in temperate climates: iron, calcium, magnesium, potassium, silica, chlorophyll, vitamin K, vitamin C, and a protein content higher than most greens. When the body reaches for nettle, it is not reaching for an instruction. It is reaching for material — the raw substance of what it needs to build and restore itself.

The body that craves nettle is a body running low. Not low in a dramatic way, necessarily — not obviously ill or depleted. But low in the quiet, cumulative way of modern life: insufficient sleep over months, the mineral losses of stress hormones, a diet that provides calories without density, a body that has been giving out more than it has been receiving. Nettle addresses this not by stimulating the body to perform better on fewer resources, but by simply providing more of what was missing.

---

#### Āyurvedic parameters

**Rasa (taste):** astringent (*kaṣāya*), bitter (*tikta*), with a sweet (*madhura*) quality that deepens when cooked  
**Guṇa (qualities):** heavy (*guru*) — more nourishing than most greens; slightly oily (*snigdha*) — it coats and feeds tissue  
**Vīrya (thermal action):** cooling (*śīta*)  
**Vipāka (post-digestive effect):** sweet (*madhura*)  
**Doṣa:** pacifies pitta through cooling and bitterness; nourishes vāta through its sweet, building qualities; can increase kapha in cold, congested constitutions if taken in very large amounts over time

The *guru* (heavy) quality is the one most worth attending to here. Most medicinal plants are light — they move, clear, stimulate, disperse. Nettle is heavy in the way that food is heavy: it builds. The *snigdha* (oily) quality means it feeds tissue rather than stripping it. This is not a plant for a body that needs to be moved along; it is a plant for a body that needs to be filled.

The astringent (*kaṣāya*) taste tones tissue — it has a gentle contracting quality that is particularly useful in conditions of excess fluid or looseness in the gut. The bitter (*tikta*) cools and supports the liver. The sweet vipāka means that despite the bitterness, the end of the plant's journey through the body is nourishment, not depletion.

---

#### The sting

Nettle stings. This is part of its nature, and worth considering rather than dismissing as an inconvenience.

The sting is the plant's signal that it is not passive. It contains histamine, formic acid, and other compounds that produce an immediate inflammatory response in the skin — and yet in dried or cooked form, taken internally, it is one of the most effective natural antihistamines available. It produces in the skin exactly what it resolves in the body. The plant that stings is the plant that knows how inflammation works from the inside.

This is not mystical. It is the kind of precise ecological knowledge that emerges from millions of years of co-evolution between plants and the bodies that eat them. Nettle knows the immune system. It knows how to provoke it locally and quiet it systemically. Respect is the appropriate response to a plant with this level of specificity.

---

#### Why the body reaches for it

Allergy season is the most visible time the body reaches for nettle — the histamine response that sits behind sneezing, watery eyes, and swollen sinuses responds well to nettle's antihistamine action. But the deeper reaching happens in spring, when the body is emerging from winter's contraction and needs to rebuild. Nettle is one of the first plants to emerge. It grows before almost anything else, dense with exactly the minerals the winter body has been burning through.

The body also reaches for nettle in states of depletion that don't have a dramatic name: the post-partum exhaustion, the slow recovery from illness, the months of overwork that have left the reserves genuinely low. In these states the body is not asking for stimulation. It is asking to be fed.

#### Preparation

**As tea:** dried nettle leaf steeped fifteen to thirty minutes in hot water produces a deeply mineralised drink. Longer steeping (sometimes called an infusion) extracts the mineral content more fully than a brief steep does. The result is darker, stronger, and more nourishing. This is not herbal tea for flavour — it is nutritional density in liquid form.

**As food:** young nettle leaves cooked exactly as spinach — blanched, sautéed, added to soups, incorporated into pasta, made into pesto. Cooking removes the sting entirely. The cooked leaf is one of the most nutrient-dense vegetables available and has been a staple spring food across cultures for millennia.

**The infusion over the tea bag:** a handful of dried nettle in a jar, covered with just-boiled water, sealed and left to steep for four to eight hours. The mineral content of a long infusion is substantially higher than a brief steep. This is nettle as medicine-food — taken daily, over seasons, as part of what the body receives rather than as an acute intervention.

---

#### A closing note

Nettle does not produce dramatic results quickly. It works in the register of sufficiency — the body that was running low begins, over weeks of use, to feel more like itself. Skin improves. Hair strengthens. The joints that were aching quietly begin to ease. Energy that felt like it was being drawn from reserves starts to feel like it is arriving from somewhere that is actually being replenished.

This is what nourishment feels like from the inside. Not stimulation. Replenishment.

The body that has been adequately fed does not need to reach.
