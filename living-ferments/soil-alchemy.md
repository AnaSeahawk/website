# Soil Alchemy

*A personal record of indoor fermentation for year-round probiotic soil building.*

---

*Bokashi* is a Japanese term for fermented organic matter. As a practice, it is an indoor, anaerobic method for processing food remnants into a pre-digested soil amendment — using lactic acid bacteria and other effective microorganisms rather than heat and oxygen.

The distinction from conventional composting is fermentation. No heat is generated. No turning is required. No methane is produced. The process is sealed and quiet. What comes out of the bin after two weeks is not compost — it is a pre-composted, acidic, probiotic material that, when buried in soil, breaks down within days and feeds the microbial community directly.

---

## Why This

The kitchen generates organic matter continuously. That matter can return to the soil it came from, or it can go to landfill.

Bokashi closes that loop indoors, year-round, without a garden. The fermented material can be buried in a pot, added to a compost pile, or carried to a patch of ground. Any of these is sufficient.

It processes most food remnants — including cooked food, meat, dairy, fish, bones — that conventional composting cannot handle safely.

---

## Method

**What is needed:**

- Two food-grade bins with tight-fitting lids, nesting inside each other
- Bokashi starter mix (bran or other absorbent material inoculated with effective microorganisms)
- Food remnants, chopped small
- A brick or similar object to raise the inner bin and create drainage space

Drill or melt 20–30 small holes in the bottom of one bin. Place the brick inside the solid bin. Set the holed bin inside it.

**Step 1 — Fermentation (10–14 days):**

Layer food remnants in the inner bin. After each layer, dust with bokashi starter. Compact down firmly and close the lid. Repeat as remnants accumulate through the day.

When the bin is full, seal it completely and set it aside for 10–14 days. Start a second bin in the meantime.

The open bin should smell slightly sour — like apple cider vinegar, perhaps a little alcoholic. Not offensive. A foul or putrid smell indicates a problem with the seal or weak starter.

**Step 2 — Soil incorporation:**

Bury the contents 15–20 cm deep in the garden or in a large pot. Up to a third of the volume can be mixed directly into the bottom of indoor or outdoor pots.

The material is acidic coming out of the bin. The pH will equilibrate with the surrounding soil within 10–14 days. Wait that window before planting directly into the amended area.

**Bokashi tea:**

Liquid collects in the outer bin during fermentation. Drain it regularly — if left too long it can build enough pressure to lock the inner and outer bins together. The tea is concentrated and must be diluted 1:100 with water before using on plants. Alternatively, it can go down the drain — it is fully natural and beneficial to drain systems and waterways.

## Making Starter Mix at Home

The starter is the culture: lactic acid bacteria, photosynthetic bacteria, and yeast distributed through an absorbent dry medium.

**Building the culture — rice water method:**

Soak rice 1:2 in water. Stir vigorously for a few minutes. Strain off the water into an open vessel. Cover with a breathable cloth and leave 5–8 days at room temperature until lightly fermented and slightly sour.

Combine 1 part fermented rice water with 10 parts whole milk. Cover and leave 10–14 days. The mixture will separate into distinct layers. Use a baster or ladle to collect the middle liquid layer. If layers do not form clearly, strain through fine mesh and keep the liquid. This is the serum.

Alternatively: kefir whey or sauerkraut juice are direct sources of the same organisms, ready to use.

**Inoculating the dry medium:**

- 50ml molasses
- 50ml serum
- 250ml warm water (pure if possible)
- 5kg dry absorbent material — wheat bran, dried leaf, coconut coir, or shredded newspaper

Combine all liquids. Pour over the dry material and mix until it holds together when squeezed but no liquid runs out. Add more water if needed. Seal in a bag or container with as little air as possible. Ferment 10–14 days.

Ready when it smells sour and slightly sweet — like apple cider vinegar. Not rotten. For long-term storage, spread it in a thin layer on shallow trays and dry in the shade before sealing.

## The Connection to Water Alchemy

The [eco-enzyme ferment](water-alchemy.md) produces two outputs at harvest: liquid and spent solids. The solids are not waste — they carry the same probiotic culture and the same organic acids. They go directly into the bokashi bin as one of the richest inputs the system can receive.

The two practices feed each other. The water ferment processes the fruit. The soil ferment processes the residue. The amended soil feeds the plants. The plants produce more fruit. The loop has no waste point.

---

## What the Soil Does with This

Probiotic soil is not a metaphor. The microbial community in fertile ground is dense, diverse, and largely fungal — mycorrhizal networks, nitrogen-fixing bacteria, LAB, organisms that produce structural compounds and hormones that plants respond to directly.

The effective microorganisms introduced through bokashi join that community. Nutrients locked in protein chains become bioavailable. pH stabilises. Water retention improves. Organic matter converts into humus rather than landfill gas.

One handful of healthy soil contains more living organisms than there are people on Earth. The kitchen has always been part of the same cycle.
