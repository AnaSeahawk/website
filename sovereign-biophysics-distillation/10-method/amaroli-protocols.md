# Amarolī Protocols

*Amaroli is the practice. Shivambu — the water of Shiva — is the substance.*

The word amaroli comes from the Sanskrit *amara*: the deathless, the immortal. The practice is described in the Hatha Yoga Pradipika and is among the oldest documented yogic disciplines. Shivambu — *shiva* (auspicious) + *ambu* (water) — names the substance itself, from the Shivambu Kalpa Vidhi of the Damar Tantra.

This document covers the practice in its raw and aged forms. For applications specific to the distilled fractions, see the companion document: [Distilled Fraction Protocols →](distilled-fraction-protocols.md)

*These protocols describe orientations within the practice, articulated relationally and without medical or therapeutic framing. Nothing here is required. Nothing is promised. The reader is invited to notice what the body receives.*

---

## Preparation

The quality of what is worked with depends on how it is handled. Preparation is part of the practice, not preliminary to it.

**Syphoning**
During aging, sediment naturally accumulates at the bottom of the vessel. Syphon from the top on an ongoing basis as this sediment forms — do not wait until the material is needed. The sediment is always discarded. What is kept is what floated clear above it. Syphoning before distillation is standard; syphoning during the aging window is the actual practice.

**Filtering**
Filtering is secondary and case-dependent. It is time-consuming and is mostly avoided in routine aging. When filtering is done, coffee filters are used — filled once and allowed to drip through, then replaced. They clog easily; the filter is not refilled or forced. When both syphoning and filtering are needed, syphon first. What passes through the filter is cleaner starting material; what remains in the filter is discarded.

For the Saturn retentate specifically, see the distilled fraction protocols.

## Fresh and Raw Practice

The practice may begin with fresh material — collected and used within hours.

1. **Morning Mid-Stream Intake**
   The practice opens with receiving the first mid-stream portion of the morning flow, when the body has been at rest.

2. **Looping**
   As the day unfolds, the practice may continue by returning to what has already passed through the body, establishing continuity.

3. **Subtle Dosing**
   The practice may also be approached lightly, through small amounts or dilution, favoring sensitivity over intensity.

## Aged Practice

Aging transforms the substance. What the Damar Tantra calls *pūtimutta* — fermented or gestated material — is a different thing from what was collected. Time is the active ingredient.

4. **Use of the Aged Portion**
   Material held over days or longer introduces time as an active element. The fermentation changes the electrochemical profile: urea breaks down into ammonia, pH rises, the character of the fluid shifts. What is used after this process is not simply stored material — it has been worked by its own biology.

5. **Saturation**
   At certain stages, engagement may deepen through increased intake, allowing the practice to permeate more fully.

6. **Periods of Fasting**
   The practice may be undertaken for a defined period using only the returning flow and water, allowing attention to settle inwardly.

7. **Infusions**
   Herbal preparations may accompany the practice, without altering its essential orientation.

## External Applications

External orientations are understood as extensions of the same field. Fresh or aged material may be used depending on context and availability.

1. **Sprays** — applied to the skin through gentle spraying.
2. **Foot Soaks** — soaking the feet, allowing gravity and time to support release.
3. **Hand Soaks** — hands immersed, easing joints and soft tissues.
4. **Facial Cleansing** — used as a facial wash, approached simply.
5. **Hair and Scalp Application** — light application to the scalp.
6. **Ear Application** — small amounts placed at the ears, guided by discernment.
7. **Eye Rinses** — diluted use applied to the eyes with care and attention.
8. **Nasal Rinse** — gentle nasal engagement, allowing the passages to clear.
9. **Oral Rinse** — held or swished in the mouth before release.
10. **Dental Application** — teeth brushed using the collected fluid as part of routine care.
11. **Enema** — rectal use, approached with readiness and restraint.
12. **Intimate Cleansing** — gentle cleansing of intimate areas. Only warm, fresh material is appropriate here — pleasant in taste and character, not aged or sharp. Emphasizing continuity rather than correction.
13. **Compresses** — cloth soaked in the collected portion applied to areas of the body.
14. **Spot Application** — the aged portion used in targeted ways for the skin.
15. **Open Applications** — direct application for minor openings of the skin, according to readiness.
16. **Support During Infection** — the aged portion used outwardly where infection is present, without promise or claim.
17. **Contemplative Use** — the practice held within meditation or prayer, without separation between inner and outer.
18. **Internal–External Continuity** — the inward practice mirrored outwardly through application over the body, maintaining a single field of attention.
19. **Immersion** — immersion in water to which the aged portion has been added extends the practice into the surrounding environment.

## Household and Environmental

The practice does not end at the body. Aged, syphoned material has documented historical application in household and agricultural contexts. The ammonia produced during fermentation is the active agent — a natural solvent, disinfectant, and cleaner that predates synthetic alternatives by millennia.

**Laundry**
Aged shivambu used as a laundry agent is among the oldest recorded applications. The Romans collected aged urine in public vessels specifically for use in fulling and laundering cloth — the ammonia lifts oils and soiling from fabric without damaging fibre. This practice continued through medieval Europe and across many other traditions. The application is straightforward: aged, syphoned material used directly in the wash.

**General Cleaning**
Windows, surfaces, and general household cleaning. The same solvent properties that make it effective in laundry make it effective here. No dilution formula is offered — this is a practice of direct observation and adjustment.

**Leather**
Urine has been used in leather preparation and tanning across cultures and throughout recorded history. The enzymatic and ammonia content softens and conditions the hide.

**Construction**
Aged urine was used as a binding and setting agent in early earthen construction — mortar, adobe, and similar applications — before synthetic cement existed. The chemical mechanism involves the ammonia reacting with lime and mineral compounds. The historical record on this is less completely sourced than the laundry and leather traditions; it is noted here as documented but incompletely mapped.

**Gardening and Agriculture**
The collected portion may be returned to the earth — diluted for plants, applied to compost, or used to condition soil.

## Advanced Practice

These orientations are named for completeness and are approached only with maturity.

1. **Śivāmbhu Yoga** — breath and amaroli paired, emphasizing retention and release.
2. **Sauna Application** — the practice extended into steam environments.
3. **Extended Aging** — portions allowed to mature over longer periods, introducing deeper fermentation.
4. **Sun and Moon Exposure** — the collected portion exposed to sunlight or moonlight before use, integrating light and time. Solar exposure is activating; lunar exposure is quieter, more receptive. Both are legitimate orientations depending on what the practice is asking for.
5. **Infused Fabrics** — cloth soaked in the collected portion worn, allowing prolonged contact.
6. **Culinary Inclusion** — small amounts included in food or broths, without emphasis. Aged material diluted in pure water may also be used for washing or soaking grains, nuts, and seeds before cooking — the same preparatory step the Solar distillate is used for, here with aged rather than distilled material.
7. **Spiritual Orientation** — the practice held within a wider contemplative life.
