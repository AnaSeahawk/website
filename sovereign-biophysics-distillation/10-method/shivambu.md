## A New Name for a New Relationship

The word most people know for this substance is not a neutral description. It carries what was installed: clinical detachment on one side, disgust on the other, and between them a wall that closes the question before it can be asked. That is not an accident. It is how erasure works — a word does the work of a lock. [The Language of Erasure →](../00-orientation/the-language-of-erasure.md) describes the mechanism in full. Here the practical consequence is simply this: the word you use shapes the relationship you have.

There are other words. There have always been other words — in every tradition that carried this knowledge, in every community that practices today, there is a name that holds the substance differently.

*Shivambu* — from the Sanskrit, the water of Shiva. The name given in the Damar Tantra, the primary text of this practice. Shiva: the principle of transformation, the third force in the Vedic trinity, the one who neither creates nor maintains but dissolves and liberates. Shiva's water: the fluid that completes the cycle.

*Amaroli* — from *amara*, immortal. The name of the practice itself in the yogic tradition. The same root as ambrosia, as amrita, as the nectar of the gods. Deathless. What the name encodes about the substance is not decoration.

*Aqua vitae* — the water of life in the alchemical tradition. The quintessence freed from its gross matrix. The sovereign healing substance the laboratory was designed to produce. In Sumerian: *me-ê ḫayyātim* — the water of life, what the two figures bring to restore Inanna after three days on the hook.

*Amṛta* — the deathless nectar of the Vedic tradition, the drink of the gods.

*Pūtimutta* — fermented urine, in Pali. The Buddha's medicine, named as one of the four foundational supports of the ordained life: "For the rest of your life you are to endeavour at that."

*The golden fountain. The living waters. The mumia* — Paracelsus's name for the vital force present and active in the body's own fluid, the living signature of the body that produced it.

In community, in conversation, in public spaces where the plain word would close doors before they open: there are options. Shivambu travels well. Living waters says what it means without saying what people expect to hear. In French-speaking communities, *eau d'or* — golden water. The name you choose is already a relationship with the substance. Choose one that lets you think about it clearly.

## What You Are Working With

The fluid is a blood derivative. That is the foundational observation — not poetic framing but anatomical fact. The kidneys filter the blood continuously. What they release is what the blood carried: the hormonal communication the body completed, the minerals at whatever ratio the body just balanced, the peptides, the metabolic record of what was eaten and felt and processed since the last collection. The kidneys do not eject this carelessly. They filter it according to what the body needs at that concentration in the blood at that moment, and they release the rest.

The pregnancy test works because something real is travelling in the fluid. hCG — the hormone of early pregnancy — is produced by the body, carried in the blood, filtered by the kidneys, and readable in what is released. When people take plant medicines, the metabolites travel the same path: blood, kidneys, fluid — still present and detectable for days. This is not metaphor. The fluid is the blood's record of the body's experience.

In an Ayurvedic frame, the fluid carries the imprint of the body's current state — the quality of the agni, the balance of the doshas, the condition of the ojas. It is plasma-level information that has already been worked by the body's intelligence. Not waste. Leftovers, in the best sense — what the blood had in excess of what it needed at that moment, released into the fluid while still carrying the signature of what the blood was doing when it was produced.

What is in the jar is a result of what was put into the body: food, water, thought, emotion. Not a list of ingredients but an energy — the body's current state made liquid and readable. That is enough to know to begin.

## Where to Begin

The Damar Tantra is specific: the midstream of the first morning collection, drunk willingly and joyfully.

Not the first stream — that carries what the urethra has held through the night. Not the last — that comes as the flow slows and the composition shifts. Midstream: the clear middle of the cycle, the most consistent and the cleanest. The morning collection is the most concentrated — the body has been filtering through the night without addition, and what is in it is the night's work.

*Willingly and joyfully* is the instruction. For some people the willingness is already there and enthusiasm follows naturally. For others there is a moment of gathering — not force, but the deliberate choosing to engage. Both are a beginning. What moves the practice forward is the relationship, and relationships begin with turning toward.

Make something of the first time. A quiet morning. A clean vessel. A few minutes of stillness before. The Damar Tantra encodes the practice as sacred transmission because it understood what it was asking of the person beginning — not a casual experiment but a decision to enter into a different relationship with the body's own intelligence. That framing is not obligatory. It is offered as one way to hold the threshold: something deliberate, something chosen, something you came to of your own accord.

There is no wrong way. There is no protocol to follow. This is not an extreme sport. What follows are suggestions from years of practice and from the community that has gathered around this work — many people, many starting points, every story unique. You are in charge here. Your body is the one you are working with, and the pace is its.

## A Natural Progression

Most people find their way to something like this, in some order:

A few drops on the back of the hand — the temperature, the texture, how the skin receives it. The soles of the feet before sleep, rubbed in and left to absorb. A drop held in the mouth. A foot bath with a small amount in warm water, twenty minutes. These are the oldest documented applications of this material — topical use goes back further than internal use in the written record and requires nothing but the willingness to make contact.

Some people spend months or years in this register before drinking. Some drink from the first day. Both are right. The pace is the body's and it will signal when it is ready for the next step.

The consumption — drinking — happens when the body has arrived at it, not before. There is no timeline and no standard. The curiosity that draws you deeper is the guide.

## The Social Reality

Many people who practice Shivambu do so in secret. They have not told their families. They have not told their partners. They carry this knowledge privately and navigate its implications in their own time. That is not ideal — ideally this practice would have the social container it deserves — but it is the workable reality of where we are, and many people do it successfully in exactly these conditions.

A private practice is a complete practice. Many of the most significant things a person does with their own body and their own inner life are never witnessed by anyone else — they do not need to be. The practice belongs to you first. What you share of it, with whom, and when, is yours to decide. Begin in the space that is already yours, tend it there, and let it grow into visibility only as that feels right.

The names help here. Shivambu, living waters, the golden fountain — language that can travel in spaces where the unadorned word would not. Communities around the world have been finding these languages for a long time. You are not alone in navigating this.

This is also why private practitioner communities exist — and why they are worth finding. Not communities built on secrecy or shame, but spaces where people speak from actual experience, where questions can be asked without having to first defend the premise, and where the conversation does not have to pass through the noise of those who have never engaged with the practice and are responding only to the idea of it. The quality of that exchange is different. There is a shorthand available between people who have sat with the same jar, noticed the same things, and had the same quiet realisations. That community is already here, and it is growing.

## What Varies

The character of the fluid varies enormously: with diet, with emotion, with medication, with time of day, with where you are in your cycle, with sleep, with illness, with season. The morning of a fever is different from the morning after deep rest. The collection during a difficult period is different from the collection after stillness.

All of this variation is data, not error. The jar records what the body is doing. Learning to read the variation — the colour, the clarity, the smell, the taste, how the skin receives it — is part of the practice, and it deepens over time.

Practitioners in this community eat different diets. Some eat meat. Most eat sodium chloride. Some have arrived at the specific dietary conditions documented elsewhere in this archive; most have not. The common thread is not diet. The common thread is the return of the fluid, sustained over time.

## Where This Leads

Amaroli — the practice of returning the body's own fluid — is complete in itself. It requires no equipment, no dietary change, no special conditions. Many people practice this for years without distilling, without strict dietary change, without any of the advanced work this archive documents. What they find fills their own record.

For the full range of Amaroli practice — aged material, topical preparations, external applications, household uses, the historical record — the dedicated document is [Amaroli Protocols →](amaroli-protocols.md).

Distillation is a deeper layer of the same work: the separation of the fluid into its fractions, each with distinct properties, each workable in different ways. It is one direction some practitioners move when the practice is established and the curiosity draws them there. It is not the required next step. There is no required next step. There is only the practice you have, the body you are working with, and what you find.
