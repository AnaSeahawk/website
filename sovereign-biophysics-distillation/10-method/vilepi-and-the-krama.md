# Vilepi and the Permanent Krama
## The Ayurvedic Re-Feeding Sequence and the Diet It Describes

---

*What the roasted grain and ghee tradition knows is not only how to feed a body in the field. It is how to feed a body that is already running its own refinement.*

---

## Vilepi: The Food That Knows Where the Body Is

What I made in my kitchen is not merely similar to an ancient therapeutic food. It is, structurally and functionally, a variant of one of the four foundational therapeutic gruels codified in classical Ayurvedic medicine.

The Sushruta Samhita — compiled in the 4th century CE from the teachings of the physician Divodasa, and the Charaka Samhita dating back as far as 1500 BCE — both document the **Kritanna Varga**: the hierarchy of cooked grain preparations used to rebuild a body that has been depleted, cleansed, or pushed to its limits. This is not a dietary guideline in the modern sense. It is a medicine system built around grain, water, and the graduated restoration of the body's own fire.

The four primary gruels of the Samsarjana Krama — the graduated re-feeding protocol used after Panchakarma and serious illness — move along a single axis: from most watery to most solid, from lightest to most nourishing, from the thinnest possible demand on the digestive system to the fullness of rebuilt strength. They are:

**Manda** — grain boiled in 14 parts water, then strained. Almost entirely liquid. The ghost of grain rather than grain itself. Given first, when the digestive fire has been reduced to an ember.

**Peya** — the same ratio, but the grain is left in. Thin, mostly water, a suggestion of solidity. The first food that asks the gut to work, however gently.

**Vilepi** — grain in 4 parts water, cooked until the particles break and the preparation thickens to a true porridge consistency. Less liquid than solid. The first food that *builds*. Described in the classical texts as **tarpani** (satiating), **brumhani** (nourishing — literally that which causes the body to fill out), **hrudya** (heart-friendly, cordial to the whole system), and **pitta nashini** (balancing to the fire element when it has burned too hot or too erratically). In the Samsarjana Krama, vilepi is introduced on the third day — the first moment the system is considered ready to receive genuine nourishment rather than maintenance fluids.

**Odana** — fully cooked rice, the return to ordinary food and the signal that the body has rebuilt its capacity to receive the world.

This sequence is not arbitrary. It is a map of how the digestive intelligence recovers: first re-wet, then gently occupied, then truly fed, then returned to participation in ordinary life. Each preparation can be enhanced — ghee can be added to vilepi, herbs can be introduced, the grain can be varied — but the essential logic is the ratio of grain to water and what that ratio asks of the body.

The roasted wheat ball — dry-heat processed flour bound with ghee and warm milk — is a dry-heat variant of vilepi. Where the classical preparation uses wet heat (boiling), the roasting accomplishes the same pre-digestion through direct fire: the grain is broken open before it ever meets liquid. The ghee corresponds exactly to the ghee noted as optional in the classical preparation, and serves the same function — to carry fat-soluble nourishment into the tissues and slow the release of grain energy. The warm milk is the water element, the *jala mahabhuta*, that allows transformation and carries mineral intelligence into circulation.

What is remarkable is that the Sherpa field preparation — tsampa kneaded with butter tea — and the classical Ayurvedic vilepi are describing the same body of knowledge from different directions. One is the high-altitude mountain food of a people who never codified it in Sanskrit. The other is the clinical food of a medical tradition that never climbed Everest. Both arrived at: pre-transform the grain, bind it with animal fat, thin it with warm liquid, present it to a body that needs to work hard with minimum digestive expenditure.

The knowledge was never lost. It simply exists in two forms — the living practice and the written text — waiting to be recognised as the same thing.

## The Ayurvedic Frame: What Is Actually Happening in the Body

The vilepi preparation — and its variants across traditions — operates through a precise Ayurvedic logic that the classical texts make explicit.

Western nutritional science measures what it can extract — the protein content, the mineral assay, the fiber percentage, the glycemic index number — and then constructs a model from the measurements. The model is real but partial. It describes the ash at the bottom of the crucible, not the fire that burned. Protein isolated from its food matrix is not the same intelligence as protein embedded in living grain bound with living fat and warm liquid. The analysis removes it from context and then wonders why the results don't replicate in the living body.

Ayurveda approaches food through a different set of questions: What is this food's quality? What does it ask of the body to process it? What does it leave behind? Is it building tissue, clearing channels, stabilizing the nervous system, or overstimulating and depleting?

In this framework, roasted barley or wheat is understood as **laghu** (light), **ruksha** (mildly drying), and **ushna** (warming by nature of the roasting). It is specifically recommended for states of high physical demand because it does not burden the agni — the digestive fire does not have to waste its force on breaking down what has already been transformed by the outer fire of roasting. The body receives the grain's intelligence without the metabolic tax.

Barley specifically carries a further Ayurvedic distinction worth naming: it is considered both **laghu** (light) and **grahi** (absorbent — meaning it binds excess moisture and ama in the gut without depleting essential fluids). This makes it unusual among grains. Most grains are either nourishing and heavy, or light and drying. Barley nourishes without adding heaviness, and its beta-glucan fiber binds what should not circulate while allowing what should to pass. The classical texts prescribed barley preparations for fever, convalescence, and any state where the digestive fire has been compromised — exactly the conditions of altitude, extreme exertion, or the body in transition.

Ghee modifies these qualities. It adds **snigdha** (unctuous, oleaginous quality), which in Ayurvedic understanding means it nourishes the deepest tissues — the **ojas**, the refined essence of vitality that sustains immunity, endurance, and the nervous system's stability. Ghee in Ayurveda is specifically characterised as **dipana** — that which kindles the digestive fire — and **rasayana** — that which rejuvenates tissue over time. Where the grain provides the fuel, the ghee nourishes the vessel and keeps it lit. This is not metaphor. Butyric acid — the short-chain fatty acid concentrated in ghee — is measurably the preferred fuel of colonocytes, the cells that line the large intestine. A gut that is well-fed at its own cellular level maintains the integrity of its barrier, absorbs nutrition efficiently, and does not generate the inflammatory signals that undermine systemic performance.

The warm liquid — milk, tea, broth — provides the **jala mahabhuta** — the water element that allows transformation, that carries minerals into circulation, that maintains the fluid environment the tissues require to function. In the Samsarjana Krama, the gruels are always served warm. Cold liquid contracts and slows. Warm liquid opens and allows. At altitude, where dehydration is a constant background threat and the cold itself depletes vital moisture from the respiratory tract with every breath, this is not incidental.

The absence of sodium chloride is worth holding precisely. Industrial salt as a food additive is not a neutral substance — it is a concentrated and isolated mineral compound that the body must process, and in the quantities embedded in processed food, it creates a physiological dependency: the kidneys adapt to excreting high sodium loads, and when that sodium is withdrawn, a period of dysregulation follows. The modern palate requires it because the modern palate has been conditioned by industrial flavouring to register plain food as insufficient. The classical preparations do not require it because they are complete without it. Vilepi, in the Samsarjana sequence, is offered with or without salt according to the patient's condition — it is not structural to the food. It is a modifier, used when the specific situation calls for it, not a default addition that every body requires at every meal.

## The Map Laid Over What Already Exists

None of this requires translation into the practice. It arrives already translated, because the ambrosia diet — warm milk, yoghurt, ghee, no sodium chloride, vegetarian, sourced from the cow — is not a parallel tradition to what the Ayurvedic gruel sequence describes. It *is* that sequence, lived permanently rather than deployed as recovery protocol.

The Samsarjana Krama was designed to rebuild a body after Panchakarma — the deep cleansing that strips accumulated toxins and simultaneously depletes the system's capacity to process heavy food. The logic of the sequence is: give the body only what it can receive without effort, and graduate that only as the digestive fire demonstrates it is ready for more. The gruels are the bridge between depletion and full nourishment. What the classical texts treat as a temporary post-therapeutic passage, the ambrosia diet treats as the permanent operating mode — because the practice itself maintains the body in a state of continuous refinement. There is no depletion phase followed by rebuilding. There is ongoing distillation, ongoing return, ongoing lightness of input matched to high quality of output.

Consider what the ambrosia diet already contains and where each element sits in the classical structure:

**Warm milk** is peya made whole — the grain removed, the liquid intelligence of the cow retained in its most elemental form. In the Samsarjana sequence, peya is the second food given to the body coming back to itself: light, hydrating, mineral-rich, asking almost nothing of the digestive fire. As a daily foundation, warm milk maintains the gut in exactly this state of gentle, continuous reception. It does not burden. It nourishes at the channel level — the *srotas* — before it reaches the deeper tissues. The classical texts are unambiguous that milk is the supreme rasayana: the substance most aligned with ojas, the body's refined vital reserve. This is not because milk is nutritionally complex in the analytical sense. It is because milk arrives in the body already resembling what the body most needs to sustain itself — warm, white, the fluid of sustenance itself.

**Yoghurt** is the cultured intelligence of milk — milk that has been worked by living organisms, its lactose partially pre-digested, its proteins made more available, its pH shifted toward the mildly acidic in a way that supports the gut's own microbial community. In the Ayurvedic framework this is the *samskara* — the transformation — that changes not the substance but its relationship to the body receiving it. Fermented milk is understood as more agni-kindling than fresh milk precisely because it has already undergone one round of biological transformation. It carries that transformative intelligence into the gut. Modern microbiome research validates this through the specific language of short-chain fatty acid production and microbial diversity — the same outcomes the Ayurvedic texts describe as the restoration of the digestive fire and the clearing of the *srotas*. Different vocabulary, same observation.

**Ghee** sits at the centre of every stage of the classical sequence. It is added to vilepi as an enhancement. It is prescribed for convalescence. It is the primary vehicle of the rasayana tradition — the preparation that carries medicinal intelligence into the deepest tissues. In the ambrosia diet it is not an enhancement. It is structural. And here is where the loop closes most completely: the cow produces milk. The milk is cultured into yoghurt. The yoghurt, or cream, is churned and clarified into ghee. The ghee enters the body and nourishes the tissues that produce the body's own distillate. The internal practice returns the refined product of those tissues back through every available channel. Nothing is lost from the circuit. The cow is not a food source. She is a co-practitioner in the loop.

**The roasted grain** — what the Himalayan tradition calls tsampa, what the Sushruta Samhita codified as vilepi — arrives now as the natural fourth element. In the ambrosia diet it fills the precise role that the classical sequence assigns to the third-stage gruel: the first food that *builds* rather than maintains, the first food that asks the tissues to receive genuine nourishment and grow stronger. Roasted wheat flour with ghee and warm milk is vilepi adapted for a practitioner who already lives at the vilepi stage of refinement — dry-heat processed for maximum digestive ease, bound with the fat that the ambrosia diet already runs on, thinned with the warm milk that is already the daily foundation. It is not a new addition. It is the grain expression of what the rest of the diet already is.

What is absent from the ambrosia diet — sodium chloride, refined sugar, industrial additives, heavy animal proteins, the enormous digestive burden of a modern omnivorous diet — is exactly what is absent from the Samsarjana Krama. Not because the sequence forbids these things in principle, but because the body coming back to itself through the gruels simply does not need them. When the digestive fire is clear and the channels are open and the tissues are receiving what they require, the cravings that drive heavy, stimulating food disappear not through willpower but through sufficiency. The body makes this plain: once it is genuinely well nourished, it becomes easy not to want the other things. The gruel sequence in classical Ayurveda was designed to produce precisely that state. The ambrosia diet maintains it continuously.

**The Shivambu practice** is the dimension the Samsarjana Krama does not speak to, because it is the dimension that requires a body already refined enough to work with — and the classical texts were addressing bodies in ordinary states of illness and recovery, not practitioners in advanced refinement. But structurally, the internal distillate closes the loop in a way the external diet alone cannot. The body receiving warm milk, yoghurt, and ghee is a body that produces a specific quality of internal chemistry — its distillate carries the refinement of the input. When that distillate is returned, the circuit doubles back on itself. The refining intelligence meets the refined body and deepens it further. This is the ouroboros not as symbol but as functional description: the body distilling itself, feeding its own distillate back, becoming progressively cleaner in its input requirements and progressively richer in its internal output.

The vilepi — the shortbread that needs no recipe — belongs to this system not as an addition but as a recognition. The grain that the Himalayan porter carried across the pass and the grain that the Ayurvedic physician prescribed after deep cleansing and the grain I roasted in my kitchen and bound with ghee and warm milk are the same understanding arriving from three different directions. It tastes like what it is: a food that knows where it is going, and trusts the body receiving it to do the rest.

## The Krama in Both Directions

The Samsarjana Krama moves in one direction in the classical texts: from manda toward odana, from depletion toward restoration, from the ember toward the restored flame. This is what the protocol describes and what the protocol was designed to do.

But the principle the krama enacts — agni responding accurately to what can genuinely be received and transformed — is not directional. When agni is sama, balanced and clear, it reports honestly in both directions: whether the body needs more, or whether it has arrived at a sufficiency from within that requires less. The practitioner who finds the ambrosia diet asking for even less over time is not experiencing depletion. They are experiencing the krama running the other way — toward progressive refinement of input as the internal production deepens. The same agni intelligence. The opposite movement.

The key to reading which direction is operative is the four states of agni: sama (balanced, trustworthy signals), vishama (variable, unreliable), tikshna (sharp, burning through reserves), manda (slow, unable to transform). A body moving toward less food from sama agni is a body in genuine sufficiency. A body moving toward less food from manda agni is a body in accumulation and shutdown. The word "less" is the same. The underlying state is entirely different.
