## The Shortbread That Needs No Recipe

I roasted wheat flour in a dry pan. The kitchen changed — that nutty, warm, almost caramel scent that comes only from heat meeting starch without water to diffuse it. Then ghee, then warm milk. Rolled into balls. And it tasted like shortbread without the sugar, without the sodium chloride, without any of the modern additions we have been told are necessary for palatability, for nutrition, for life.

What I made has a ten-thousand-year history, though it goes by different names in different mountains.

In Tibet and the high Himalayas it is called **tsampa** — roasted barley flour kneaded with yak butter tea and sometimes dried cheese, pressed into dense dough balls with the palm of the hand. In the high passes of the Indian subcontinent and across into the steppes, near-identical preparations appear under different local names. In ancient Greece and Rome, the soldiers and gladiators of the pre-Christian era ate roasted barley grain — *alphita* — sustained on this alone for days of marching. What I held in my hand belongs to this lineage. It is not a substitute for modern food. It is prior to it. It is what food looked like before it was mediated by industry, by supplement science, by the grammar of macronutrients.

The wheat variant I made is the European cousin of the Himalayan original. Where barley was the highland grain of Asia — perfectly suited to thin air and short growing seasons, thriving where rice and wheat failed — wheat served the same function in the Alpine world, roasted into what various traditions kept as travel bread: dense, shelf-stable, requiring nothing but a fire and a hand to prepare. The shared principle is identical across these unconnected lineages: **roast the grain, bind it with animal fat, shape it with the hands, carry it into terrain where complex food is impossible.**

## What the Roasting Does

This is not incidental technique. The roasting transforms the food at a fundamental level, and it is here that modern nutritional science, despite its vocabulary, lags behind what mountain peoples simply knew.

When grain is dry-roasted, the heat begins to break down the complex starch chains without water — a process called **dextrinization**. The long glucose polymers shorten. The grain partially pre-digests itself in the pan before it ever enters the body. This means that when tsampa or a roasted wheat ball is consumed, the digestive tract receives something it can begin to work with almost immediately, without the full enzymatic burden of processing raw or conventionally cooked starch. At altitude — where digestion slows, appetite suppresses, and the gut is under the physiological stress of hypoxia — this is not a minor convenience. It is the difference between the body being able to extract fuel and not.

In Ayurvedic understanding, this is the relationship between food and **agni** — the digestive fire. The roasting pre-activates what Ayurveda understands as a food's *pranic potential*: its readiness to be transformed. A food that is heavy and raw demands that the body's own fire spend itself in the work of transformation. A food that has already met fire — dry, direct, without the buffer of water — arrives already partially opened. It asks less of the agni and gives more. This is why barley is specifically noted in Ayurvedic texts as a food for times of physical stress, seasonal transition, and weakened digestion: it is considered **light** (laghu) in Ayurvedic quality despite being a grain, because roasted barley is easy for the digestive system to handle. The body does not have to fight for it.

The ghee does something equally precise. In Ayurvedic understanding, ghee is not merely a fat — it is **dipana** (that which kindles) and **rasayana** (that which rejuvenates tissue). It carries the memory of milk, the refinement of clarification, the transformation of heat applied to cream until all water and milk solids are driven away and only the clear, golden lipid intelligence remains. Modern biochemistry would note the short-chain and medium-chain fatty acids in ghee — butyrate particularly, which is fuel for the cells lining the intestinal wall — and the fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, K that it carries. What this means in the body is warmth in the literal thermogenic sense, lubrication of the gut lining, and a slower release of the grain's energy into the bloodstream.

Grain plus animal fat is not the crude energy delivery system it is sometimes described as in modern nutritional frameworks. It is a *timing mechanism*. The fat slows the absorption of the carbohydrate. The result is not a spike and crash but a long, even burn — the kind of fuel needed for a twelve-hour day carrying weight at 4,000 metres, or for a travel day where stopping is not possible, or for a body that needs to be warm and functional in cold air far from shelter.

The warm milk — or in the Himalayan version, butter tea — adds electrolytes, minerals, and hydration in a form the body can use without having to do the work of extraction. It also activates the dough's texture, its binding, the specific hand-kneaded quality that makes it hold.

No sodium chloride is required. This is not a minor detail — it is the one that reveals most about how completely the modern food system has reclassified an additive as a necessity.

No ancient tradition declared salt a nutrient. Ayurveda, classical Chinese medicine, Haṭha yoga, the Tantric texts, the Egyptian temple priesthood, the Greek physicians — all recorded it as irritant, stimulant, or cause of degeneration. The verdict across eight unconnected traditions is consistent: salt inflames appetite, hardens tissue, disturbs the clarity required for practice, and shortens life. The body does not assimilate it — chloride enters the organism and is excreted as chloride, unchanged, after spending its transit contracting tissue and displacing fluids.

Where salt appears in traditional mountain diets, it arrived not as a freely chosen food but through the political structures that imposed salt consumption at civilisational scale. Salt monopolies were instruments of control before they were commercial operations: the Roman military *salaria* — from which the word *salary* descends — used salt as soldier's pay and dependency simultaneously; the Han Dynasty monopolies; the French gabelle; the British taxation of salt in India that Gandhi made the centre of an independence movement. The pattern across each instance is identical: whoever controlled the salt controlled the population, not only economically but physiologically. A body running on the narcotic is easier to govern than one that is not.

The shortbread taste I encountered without salt or sugar is the taste of food before the addiction economy touched it. The palate that has been through the withdrawal recognises it not as bland but as complete. That it surprised me is the signal — the palate had been trained to read plain food as insufficient, and is finding its way back to what sufficient actually is.

## The Parallel Lineages: Mountain Foods That Survive Without the Modern Framework

The conversation of roasted grain, animal fat, and minimal liquid as field food appears independently across mountain cultures that never contacted each other. This convergence is not coincidence. It is the same problem — how does a body sustain extreme physical output in cold, high-altitude terrain with limited carrying capacity and no infrastructure — arriving at the same answer through different hands.

In the Sardinian pastoral tradition, shepherds carried **pane carasau** — the twice-baked, paper-thin flatbread made from semolina and water — which could last up to a year without spoiling. It was not merely bread. It was the engineering of preservation through double desiccation, the removal of all moisture that would allow spoilage, the transformation of grain into something that could be folded into a bag and forgotten for months. When needed, it was broken and softened in broth, or eaten dry. Its density per gram of weight is among the highest of any traditional bread. This is the same problem-solving as tsampa — the elimination of water weight and spoilage, the concentration of caloric density into the most portable possible form.

Across the Alps, the same instinct appears: dense rye breads designed to last through winter, buckwheat preparations carried by shepherds moving between seasonal pastures, the prevalence of animal fat — butter, lard — as the insulating and energizing companion to grain at altitude. The specific ingredients differ because the specific biome differs. Barley grows at altitude in Asia. Rye and buckwheat hold in the European highlands. The fat is yak in Tibet, cow in the Alps, sheep in Sardinia. The principle is identical.

What unifies all of these is their *absence from the modern food system*, and what the modern food system has replaced them with: protein isolates, electrolyte powders, carbohydrate gels, vitamin-mineral supplements, all the dissected and reconstituted fragments of what was once whole. The irony is that the fragments cost more, perform less, and lose entirely the pranic quality — the *aliveness* — of the original.

## The Sherpa Body: Living Proof Against the Dogma

Here the conversation shifts from food to flesh, because the Sherpa people are themselves a piece of evidence that the entire framework of modern performance nutrition is built on assumptions derived from lowland, sedentary, industrially-fed bodies — and those assumptions do not hold universally.

The genetic research is unambiguous in its findings, even if its implications remain underappreciated. Sherpa and Tibetan highlanders carry specific variants in the gene **EPAS1** — encoding a subunit of the Hypoxia Inducible Factor pathway — that are found almost nowhere else in the human population. These variants alter the fundamental relationship between the blood, oxygen, and tissue. Where a lowlander's body responds to thin air by overproducing hemoglobin — thickening the blood, increasing the risk of clotting and cardiac stress — the Sherpa body does something more elegant: it runs efficiently on less oxygen at the cellular level. The serum erythropoietin levels in Sherpas at 3,440 metres are equivalent to those of non-Sherpa people at 1,300 metres. Their blood does not over-respond. It does not panic. It is calibrated.

But the metabolic adaptations are equally revealing, and they challenge something fundamental about modern dietary orthodoxy. Research conducted by Cambridge University and published in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* found that Sherpa skeletal muscle demonstrates a significantly lower capacity for fatty acid oxidation compared to lowlander muscle — at baseline, not just at altitude. Their mitochondria are fewer in number but more efficient in oxygen utilisation. And their preferred metabolic fuel is not fat but carbohydrate. Burning carbohydrate requires less oxygen per unit of energy produced than burning fat. In a body operating in thin air, this is not a disadvantage — it is a precision adaptation. The Sherpa body runs lean on carbohydrate because carbohydrate is the oxygen-efficient fuel. Tsampa is almost entirely complex carbohydrate with fat as a secondary component. The traditional diet and the evolved physiology are in exact correspondence.

The modern narrative of performance — high-fat, low-carbohydrate diets, fat adaptation, ketogenic approaches — is derived from lowland physiology in oxygen-rich environments and extrapolated as universal truth. The Sherpa body refuses this extrapolation. It demonstrates that the grain-and-fat food that mountain peoples have carried for ten thousand years is not a primitive placeholder waiting to be improved by supplementation. It is a system that was already solved.

There is also the question of what Sherpas and porters carry — and how. A porter in the Khumbu carrying 40 to 60 kilograms at 4,500 metres, moving steadily for eight to ten hours, is operating at a physical register that Western sports science struggles to describe because the typical research subject does not resemble such a person. The fuel for this work is tsampa, butter tea, and dal bhat. Not protein shakes. Not creatine loading. Not periodized macronutrient cycling. The bodies that perform most reliably at the edge of what humans can do in mountain terrain are sustained on the simplest possible food — pre-roasted grain, animal fat, and warm liquid — and have been for as long as the lineage holds memory.

## Bringing It Back: The Modern Application

What I made — roasted wheat flour, ghee, warm milk — is a practical entry into this tradition that requires nothing exotic, nothing expensive, nothing that cannot be prepared in a bare kitchen or over a camp stove.

The principles that make it work:

**Roast completely before adding liquid.** The roasting is not about browning the surface — it is about transforming the starch throughout the grain. A consistent medium-low heat, constant movement, until the flour deepens to a golden-tan and the nutty scent is fully present. This cannot be rushed without losing the transformation.

**The fat is structural, not garnish.** Ghee in this preparation is not added for flavour. It is the binding intelligence that slows the carbohydrate release, carries fat-soluble nutrition, lubricates the gut lining, and provides the caloric density that makes the ball worth carrying. The proportion matters — enough to bring the flour to a dough-able consistency, not so much that the ball loses structural integrity in the hand.

**Warm liquid opens the flour.** Cold liquid fights the fat. Warm milk or warm water integrates, activates, and determines the final texture. In the Himalayan version, the butter tea itself provides heat, salt, and additional fat simultaneously. Here, warm milk provides the mineral matrix and the liquid intelligence without requiring the elaborate preparation of butter tea.

**No added flavouring is required.** The shortbread quality I encountered is the actual flavour of well-roasted wheat and ghee. If sweetness is wanted, a small amount of raw honey mixed in after forming the ball is consistent with traditional variations across multiple lineages. Spice — cinnamon, cardamom — can be added before or during roasting and integrates completely into the flour. These are not improvements. They are variations that keep the principle intact.

**For travel, form small dense balls.** Palm-sized or smaller. Wrapped in cloth or tucked into a bag. They keep for days without refrigeration in moderate temperatures, longer in cool conditions. They require nothing to eat — no utensils, no hydration beyond what is already at hand, no preparation. This is the point.

## A Note on Simplicity as Intelligence

The question this experiment raises is the one that matters: if it is this simple, why is this not common knowledge?

The answer is partly commercial — simple, portable, traditional food does not have a supply chain or a profit margin. And partly epistemological — the kind of knowledge that lives in the hands, in the practice of roasting flour until the nose says it is ready, in the ratio of ghee to flour that the palms learn through repetition — this knowledge does not transmit through nutrition labels or supplement protocols. It transmits through making, through the body's own encounter with the food.

The Sherpa carrying 50 kilograms over the Khumbu Glacier does not know the beta-glucan content of his morning tsampa. He knows that the bowl of it kneaded with butter tea before first light will carry him until midday, that his body is familiar with it in the way it is familiar with breath, that the simplicity is not lack but precision. The porter who has eaten this way since childhood is not primitive. He is the practitioner of a tradition that has been refined over longer than any laboratory study — refined not by measurement but by the feedback of bodies surviving, enduring, performing in conditions that most of the modern world will never encounter.

What I made in my kitchen, with organic wheat flour and good ghee and warm milk, is a small return to that line of knowledge. It tastes like shortbread without the additions because the additions were never necessary. They were commercial. They were the noise added to disguise the absence of signal.

The signal was always in the roasted grain.

## The Two Jars

There is a distinction worth making clearly, because the Ayurvedic sequence makes it too: vilepi has its place, and this is not always it.

The warm bowl of vilepi — rice cooked down in water, served with ghee, taken slowly at home — is the foundational preparation. It belongs to stillness, to the kitchen, to water and a flame and time. It is not what I carry.

What I carry is simpler than that, and that simplicity is its own intelligence.

A small jar of roasted flour. A small jar of ghee. Nothing else required.

When there is no water, no bowl, no fire, no time — a pinch of flour pressed into the ghee with two fingers and brought to the mouth is a complete act. When I am traveling and the body asks for something without asking for a meal, this answers without ceremony. When a day of work surfaces and the idea of cooking is one demand too many but the body needs to be acknowledged — the jar of flour, the jar of ghee, a few minutes and warm milk if it is there, water if it is not, and the thing is done.

This is not replacing the vilepi. The vilepi remains the proper preparation for when proper preparation is possible. What the two jars provide is the gap that the vilepi cannot fill — the in-between moments, the travel days, the times when the ambrosia diet is present in principle but the infrastructure that supports it is not. The roasted flour does not need the cow to be present in the room. It does not need a kitchen. It asks almost nothing of the moment and gives back something real.

The Sherpa's tsampa was never the feast. It was the thing in the pocket that kept the body honest between feasts, on the pass, in the cold, when stopping was not an option. The two jars are that — the pocket preparation, the emergency staple, the small and ready thing that fills the gap without pretending to be more.

Roast a batch. Keep it sealed. Keep the ghee alongside it. That is all.
