# Experiment Report: June 17 — Creating Our Own Ocean

**Operator:** Ana Seahawk
**Date:** June 17, 2026
**Input:** Fresh household pure plasma moving through daily drinking, cooking, topical use, and distillation
**Run structure:** Five measured distilled batches; Batch A and Batch D include paired fresh pre-distillation readings

## Operator's Account: Creating Our Own Ocean

Today brought a feeling that is difficult to describe without first clarifying the role of pure plasma within our current practice.

There was an abundance moving through the day. There was enough for drinking, enough for cooking, enough for combining with milk in our warm morning infusion, enough for skin applications, enough for distillation, and enough remaining to support every part of the cycle without interruption.

We are still consuming more than we are producing.

Nothing is sitting idle.

Nothing is being stored because there is too much of it.

The aged material collected when we first arrived in Albania is now over a month old and is steadily being used through enemas and laundry. The fresh plasma is used. The distilled plasma is used. The milk is used. Everything continues to move.

What felt different today was not surplus.

It was flow.

Pure plasma remains the primary fluid in our household. It forms the foundation of the day and the medium through which most of our food is prepared.

The rhythm is now fairly consistent.

We begin with fresh plasma. After that comes a warm distilled infusion combined with milk, usually in roughly equal proportions. This is the highest concentration of milk we consume during the day.

The remainder of our meals are generally prepared with a much higher proportion of pure plasma than milk, often somewhere around eighty percent pure plasma and twenty percent milk, sometimes undiluted pure plasma is used, the exact ratios vary from meal to meal.

Our meals remain simple.

If the morning drink is counted as a meal, we generally eat three times each day. The food is built around a small collection of familiar staples: crepes, noodles, oats, bulgur, warm milk drinks and yogurt. One or more of these appears almost every day. Sometimes a crepe is served with yogurt. Ghee is added to the bowl or mug at service rather than heavily cooked into the meal itself.

Milk functions as a companion to the cycle rather than its foundation.

What felt significant today was not the quantity of anything but the ease with which everything moved.

There was enough for every purpose without any feeling of scarcity.

This led me back to the image of the ocean.

The ocean is not a reservoir in the way people often imagine. It is not a static body holding the same water indefinitely. It receives from rivers, rainfall, groundwater, atmosphere, and life itself. It gives itself back through evaporation, clouds, weather systems, currents, and countless forms of exchange.

The ocean is always changing.

We simply do not notice because it is so vast.

Our own waters seem to behave in a similar way.

The body draws continuously from its environment and returns continuously to it. Food becomes tissue fuel. Water the liquid flow becomes circulation charge. Milk becomes plasma. Plasma becomes pure plasma. Everything is moving.

The practice is not about accumulating endless reserves.

It is about learning to work consciously with circulation and charge.

Sometimes that circulation produces a healthy overflow. When it does, a portion can be set aside to age. Not because more is inherently better, but because some forms of work require time. Aged preparations cannot be created in the moment they are needed. They must be allowed to mature.

In that sense, creating overflow is not accumulation.

It is participation.

Not controlling the tide.

Working with it.

## Observation: Consumption, Selection, and Gradients Within the Day

One aspect of the measurements should be understood within the context of our daily rhythm.

Whenever fresh material is available, both of us naturally taste and evaluate it as part of the ongoing practice. We have done this from the beginning.

The relationship with fresh plasma comes first.

Throughout the day we sip and cycle what is produced, maintaining direct contact with the changing character of the output. This is not an occasional event or a special observation period. It is simply how the practice unfolds.

As a result, selection is constantly taking place.

When multiple fresh samples are available, the clearest material is usually consumed directly. Color, transparency, and visible particle density are all part of that evaluation.

This is not an afterthought and it is not a procedure created for the purpose of measurement.

It is an established part of the practice itself.

Distillation typically receives what remains after this natural process of selection and consumption has already occurred.

Because of this, the distilled batches should not be interpreted as representing an average sample of everything produced throughout the day. They often represent material that was visually denser, darker, or less appealing than other samples available at the same time.

The differences are generally obvious when viewed side by side.

What makes them difficult to describe is that they usually appear as a continuous gradient rather than as distinct categories.

At one end of the spectrum are the clearest and lightest samples, which are often chosen for direct consumption.

At the other end are darker and denser samples, which are more likely to be directed toward distillation.

Most of what is produced falls somewhere between these two extremes.

If only the opposite ends of the spectrum were compared, the differences would be immediately apparent.

Viewed throughout the day, however, the transition is gradual.

Another pattern continues to emerge.

Output often appears lighter and clearer during the night and early morning hours, while later-day output tends to become denser and less abundant. This may relate to meal timing, digestion, hydration patterns, circadian rhythm, or some combination of factors.

Because our primary meal is often taken relatively late, this observation may be connected to the body's processing cycle, although no firm conclusion can yet be drawn.

The numbers continue to vary.

The visual character varies as well.

Yet beneath those variations there remains a recognizable continuity, as though the material is moving along a spectrum rather than changing from one state into another.

That alone feels worthy of continued observation.

## Commentary on the Measurements

Several patterns stood out when comparing the batches recorded throughout the day.

The first observation is the remarkable consistency of the distilled material.

Despite substantial variation in the fresh samples, every distilled batch settled into a narrow range. Distilled pH values remained between 9.4 and 9.5 across all recorded runs. This has become a recurring signature that continues to appear regardless of differences in the source material.

The second observation is the large reduction in conductivity and dissolved solids following distillation.

Batch A began at 3950 µS/cm and 1980 ppm before distillation and finished at 1102 µS/cm and 552 ppm.

Batch D began at 3380 µS/cm and 1690 ppm before distillation and finished at 824 µS/cm and 406 ppm.

Although the fresh samples differed considerably from one another, the distilled outputs converged toward a much narrower range.

Hydrogen measurements showed the greatest variation of any parameter recorded.

The lowest hydrogen reading among the distilled samples was Batch D at 28 ppb, while the highest was Batch C at 102 ppb.

Fresh material also varied considerably. Batch A showed no measurable hydrogen, while Batch D measured 134 ppb before distillation.

Unlike pH, which remained remarkably stable after distillation, hydrogen values continued to fluctuate significantly between batches.

ORP measurements also showed variation.

The most negative fresh reading was Batch A at -85 mV, followed by Batch D at -58 mV.

Among the distilled samples, ORP ranged from approximately -14 mV to -47 mV, with Batch C producing the strongest negative value of the day among the distilled material.

Another point of interest is the apparent convergence of the distilled batches despite the ongoing selection process.

The material entering the distiller was not random. The clearest portions were generally consumed directly, while darker and denser portions were more likely to be distilled. Even with this ongoing selection, the distilled outputs continued to cluster around similar pH values and broadly similar conductivity ranges.

Viewed as a whole, the fresh samples displayed the widest variation, while the distilled samples displayed the greatest consistency.

The strongest numerical pattern observed today was not any individual reading but the tendency for diverse fresh material to produce distilled outputs with a recognizable and repeatable signature.
