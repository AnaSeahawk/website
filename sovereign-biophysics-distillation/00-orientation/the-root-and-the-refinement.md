# The Root and the Refinement

---

The archive has described the practice without naming its erotic root. That omission has a logic to it — clarity about the distillation process, the lineage, the measurement, the dietary foundation, comes before the more complex territory. But the omission is now a gap, and the gap is significant.

The practice and sexuality are not separate operations. They draw from the same root, refine the same substance, and produce — when cultivated rather than expendsed — the same medicine. The three traditions most articulate about this are the Taoist, the Ayurvedic (specifically through the Hatha Yoga Pradipika), and the Kaula Tantric. All three are saying the same thing with different vocabulary and different cultural assumptions. One practitioner's engagement with the source material, and with what the body has shown, is documented here.

---

## Jing: The Root Substance

The Chinese tradition's Three Treasures — *Jing* (Essence), *Qi* (Energy), *Shen* (Spirit) — describe a progression of refinement that runs through the body at every level. Jerry Alan Johnson, in the second volume of his *Chinese Medical Qigong Therapy*, states the alchemical core directly:

> Chinese internal alchemy, whereby Jing (Essence) is transformed into Qi (Energy), Qi is transformed into Shen (Spirit), and Shen is transformed into Wuji (infinite space) and then released back to the Dao. These metaphysical transformations described the progression in energetic evolution, from awareness of the body to the awareness of the breath; from awareness of the breath to the awareness of the mind; from awareness of the mind to the awareness of space; and from awareness of space to the awareness of the Dao.

Jing is stored primarily in the kidneys and is the most material of the three — the densest, most embodied form of the body's constitutional intelligence. It is the fluid the kidney system processes. It is the substrate the Lower Dantian works when heat is applied to it. And it is the foundation of reproductive life: the seed, the root, the constitutional inheritance.

This is the same substance described in the archive as *prima materia*. Wang Mu's synonym table in the neidan tradition lists it as True Lead, kidney water, Mother of the Elixir, Golden Liquor. The Charaka Samhita places its most refined expression — ojas — at the endpoint of the dhatvagni chain, produced when all seven tissue fires complete their work in sequence. The Hatha Yoga Pradipika calls it bindu: the drop. What unifies these names is the insistence that the most refined biological intelligence in the body has a physical substrate, and that substrate is sexual in the deepest sense — not in the limited sense of genital activity, but in the sense of being the root from which all other refinement grows.

The archive's distillation practice processes what the kidney system has already produced. The quality of what is distilled is therefore directly connected to the quality of what Jing the body is currently holding and expressing.

## The Post-Orgasm Instruction

Three traditions give the same instruction in direct terms.

The Hatha Yoga Pradipika's mudra chapter describes the practice of Amaroli: *"He who drinks Amari, snuff it daily, and practices Vajroli, is called practicing Amaroli."* Amaroli is named directly, in the same chapter that details Vajroli (semen/fluid retention during cohabitation) and Sahajoli. The text frames the three as variations of the same practice, all pointing toward the same end: the preservation and recirculation of the bindu.

*"If the hole behind the palate be stopped with Kechari by turning the tongue upwards, then bindu cannot leave its place even if a woman were embraced."* The mudra that holds the soma nectar at the crown — preventing its descent and loss — is described in the context of sexual union. The connection is not incidental. Khechari, pranayama, bindu preservation, and Amaroli are a connected system in the HYP, not isolated techniques.

The Damar Tantra's extended instructions on Shivambu preparation include the specification, across several verses, that the practice's power varies with the practitioner's state and diet. The implication running through the text — made explicit in later commentaries — is that the fluid produced following states of deep practice, including sexual states, carries a distinct charge.

The most direct contemporary statement comes from Avnayt's *First Water*, a practitioner's account written entirely outside the classical tradition but arriving at the same observation: *"For both men and women, urine taken after an orgasm is supposed to be the best. However it has to be an orgasm that was arrived through pleasure hormone release with the full holistic pleasure, rather than a mindless physical forcing that involves stress and tension, performance anxiety and so on. All of those hormones go into the urine too."*

This is an observation, not an instruction. What three independent traditions are noting is that the substrate changes with the state of the organism producing it. A body that has moved fully into the pleasure state — expanded, peripherally charged, the organism flowing outward — is producing a different fluid than a body locked in chronic contraction or performance anxiety. What Wilhelm Reich demonstrated with his oscillograph in 1934 gives this a biophysical framing: the surface charge of the organism measurably increases during genuine pleasure — not mechanical arousal, but the *streaming* of current — and decreases during anxiety and contraction. The fluid that the organism produces in those two states carries the signature of those states.

The post-orgasm instruction is not instruction. It is the observation that in a body whose practice has cultivated genuine Jing, the fluid produced at the peak of the pleasure circuit carries that cultivation in concentrated form.

## The Feminine Current

The women's tradition in Taoism — transmitted through Sun Bu-er, Tang Guangzhen, and the lineage Thomas Cleary preserved in *Immortal Sisters* — begins from a different root substance than the male neidan tradition. Where male practice works with *Jing* as semen, the women's tradition works with *xue* — blood. The cyclical production of the female body. The monthly descent.

Mantak Chia's *Sexual Reflexology: Taoist Love and Sex* states this plainly: *"For women, cultivating sexual energy is a way to transform blood into Chi. Transforming sexual energy is directly related to the menstrual cycle."*

This is the women's alchemical sequence: blood → chi → shen. The parallel of men's: semen → chi → shen. Different roots, the same refinement chain.

The menstrual fluid is not waste in this framework. It is the body's most concentrated production, carrying the hormonal, immunological, and genetic signature of the current cycle. Avnayt's account, written from a practitioner's lived observation, describes what the tradition gestures at: menstrual blood as *"the highest form of concentrated human energy,"* carrying the genetic code and the ancestral field in a form distinct from what urine alone carries. The monthly variation in urine itself — documented in the archive's Avnayt material — reflects the cycle's phases: the pre-period fluid dense with fat-carried hormones, the period fluid charged differently, the non-period fluid cleaner and more forward-facing. The body is running a monthly alchemy, and every phase of it produces a distinct substance.

The Kaula Tantric tradition — documented in the Yoni Tantra's translation by Michael Magee — makes the combined fluid explicit as a formal practice: *yoni tattva*, the mixture of menses and semen. The text's introduction quotes the Kaulajnananirnaya: *"In Kaula Agama, the five pure and eternal substances are ash, wife's nectar, semen, menstrual blood and ghee mixed together."* And more directly: *"The sādhaka should mix the effusion from yoni and liṅga in water, sipping this amrita, he should nourish himself with it."*

This is not a fringe practice. It is presented in the Kaula tradition as the highest practice — not despite its biological directness, but because of it. The tradition understood that the combined biological intelligences of two cultivated bodies produce something neither could produce alone. The yoni tattva is not the encounter of two random fluids. It is the encounter of two distinct refinement chains — one working from the root of Jing-as-semen, one working from the root of xue-as-blood — and their combination in a single cup described as *amrita*.

## The Partner Circuit

What happens to the practice when another body enters the field is not systematically documented in the archive. The three-year topical observations note the partner dimension. The Current State of the Work describes the relational context without theorising it. This piece names what the tradition suggests.

Two practitioners engaged in genuine Jing cultivation are not simply sharing biology. Each body is refining the same root substance through different alchemical chains. The man's cultivation modifies the quality of his semen across days and weeks of sustained practice — Wang Mu is explicit that the Elixir is refined continuously, not produced in a single act. The woman's practice modifies the quality of her monthly output and her sexual fluid. When two bodies whose practices have been running converge, the combined fluid carries both refinement histories.

Avnayt frames this in terms of the energy field rather than the biochemistry: *"His semen has the entire genetic code of humanity fine tuned for the particular environment that the woman's body is."* The biological mirroring she describes — the woman's body capturing and reflecting the man's field, returning it in modified form — is precisely what the closed-loop principle of circulatio describes at the individual level: the substance returned to the body carries what the body most needs to recognise about itself. In partnership, the same principle operates across two systems: each body is returned a reflection of itself by the other, modified by the other's cultivation.

The practice does not require a partner. It is complete as a single-body operation. But the theory of what becomes available when two cultivated bodies are in circuit — and when the combined fluid is treated as the archive treats all its fractions, as something worthy of careful attention rather than dismissal — is present in the tradition and absent from the archive's current record. This is noted as an open direction rather than a documented finding.

## What the Crown Knows

The Hatha Yoga Pradipika's most striking instruction on soma is in the khechari mudra chapter:

> Immortal liquor is the nectar exuding from the moon (*Chandra* situated on the left side of the space between the eyebrows). It is produced by the fire which is generated by thrusting the tongue.

And: *"He who drinks the clear stream of liquor of the moon falling from the brain to the sixteen-petalled lotus in the heart... becomes free from disease and tender in body, like the stalk of a lotus, and the Yogi lives a very long life."*

The soma that descends from the crown in this practice is not symbolic. The HYP is describing a physiological event: the activation of the body's highest distillation chamber, releasing a fluid downward that the lower practice then circulates. The Taoist tradition's *Shen* — the third treasure, what Jing becomes when the refinement chain completes — is the equivalent. It is what the body produces at its most refined, and it manifests as a quality of awareness: medhagni, the flame of intelligence and perception, that can discriminate without effort between what is real and what is rationalised.

The archive's Current State documents this transition directly, without the vocabulary to fully name it: the removal of cannabis and the introduction of the distilled fractions produced a sustained return of lucid dreaming at an intensity not present in this practitioner's adult life. The correlation is direct. What the tradition names as shen activation — the crown beginning to flow — the body experienced as the night half of the practice opening. The physical and the subtle are not separate operations. The Jing that is cultivated by practice, diet, and the recirculation of the fractions eventually reaches a threshold at which Shen activates. The crown opens. The dreaming deepens.

This is the end state the practice is working toward. It is not an epiphenomenon of the practice. It is its completion.

## The Circuit at Full Extension

The archive describes a closed-loop circulatio: the body's most refined output returned to the body as input, repeatedly, across years of practice. What this piece adds is the understanding that the circuit's root is erotic.

Jing — the constitutional essence that sits at the base of the Three Treasures — is the seed of both sexual life and alchemical refinement. They are not two separate applications of the same substance. They are the same process, operating at different scales of the same body. Cultivated sexuality does not diminish what is available for distillation. It refines the substrate further. And the practice's fractions, returned to the body through the amaroli circuit, create the conditions under which Jing itself is less depleted and more refined — producing, in turn, a different quality of substance for the next cycle.

The ouroboros. The serpent eating its own tail. In the erotic body, this is not a symbol. It is the operational description of what happens when the practice is understood at its root.
