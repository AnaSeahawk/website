# The Language of Erasure
## How a Word Closes a Question Before It Can Be Asked

---

*The body was not the only thing taken. The language that would have allowed it to be seen was taken first.*

---

## The Mechanism

There is a process by which a question stops being asked.

Not through argument. Not through evidence. Not through anything that engages the critical faculty at all — because the critical faculty is precisely what the process bypasses.

The process is this: a word is repeated, by trusted voices, in authoritative contexts, with sufficient frequency, until it is no longer a word that carries a question. It becomes a word that closes one. The mind, encountering the word, does not evaluate. It classifies. The question that might have arisen — *what is this? what does it do? what is it for?* — does not form. The category has already been assigned. The label does its work before the inquiry begins.

This is not propaganda in the dramatic sense. It does not require a campaign, a plan, a conspiracy, or even awareness of what it is doing. It is the ordinary mechanics of how suggestion installs itself in a culture. Robert Temple, writing about the psychology of hypnosis, called this the bypass mechanism: the method by which input is accepted without evaluation. In formal hypnosis, the bypass is induced deliberately — through focused attention, repetition, authority, emotional arousal. In everyday life, the same mechanism operates continuously, without induction, without ceremony, simply through the accumulated weight of what is said often enough by people we are conditioned to trust.

The question *is this waste?* stops arising because the word *waste* has already answered it.

The question *is this sacred?* stops arising because the word *primitive* has already answered it.

The question *what does my body know?* stops arising because the institutional framework that replaced the question has already told us: the body does not know. The body is managed. The body is monitored. The body is the object of expertise held elsewhere.

This is the mechanism. It is not sinister in its individual instances. It is devastating in its cumulative effect — because what it closes is not just a question but the entire category of inquiry that the question belonged to.

## What Was Named

The substances the female body produces carry specific names in the traditions that understood them. Names that locate them within a pharmacological and cosmological framework — that place them in relationship to the body's other processes, to the lunar cycle, to the food that produced them, to the woman who made them.

*Soma* — the hidden fluid, the lunar substance, the concentrated intelligence of what the body processed. The Vedic tradition built an entire cosmology around this name because the substance required a name that could hold its significance.

*Me-ê ḫayyātim* — the water of life, the living waters, the substance that restored Inanna after the three days of dissolution. A Sumerian name that encoded both the biological reality and the mythological function simultaneously.

*Aqua vitae* — the water of life in the alchemical tradition. The distilled quintessence, the fifth element freed from its gross matrix, the sovereign healing substance that the laboratory was designed to produce.

*Mumia* — Paracelsus's name for the vital force present and active in the body's own fluid. The living signature. Not the fluid itself but what it carries — the biological intelligence encoded in what the body produced.

*Prima materia* — first matter, the humble and overlooked substance from which the Great Work begins. Hidden in plain sight. Found everywhere. Valued by almost no one.

Each of these names is an invitation to inquiry. Each one places the substance inside a framework of meaning that makes the question *what is this?* not only possible but urgent. Each one asks the person who holds it to pay attention, to observe, to bring their own intelligence to what the body has made.

Then the naming changed.

One word. Repeated in medical schools, in hospitals, in product advertising, in hygiene education delivered to children, in the clinical language absorbed by nurses and doctors and mothers and daughters across generations. A word that does not invite inquiry. A word that closes it.

*Waste.*

## How Suggestion Works on a Vocabulary

Temple's analysis of social hypnosis is precise on this point: the collective trance is not maintained by a single operator. It is emergent — arising from the accumulated weight of suggestion operating across multiple channels simultaneously, none of which needs to know what the others are doing. The characteristics of the trance state include reduced critical faculty around certain subjects, automatic behavioral compliance, and confabulated memory: the filling of gaps with plausible material that feels like knowledge but is installed suggestion.

The woman who has been taught from girlhood that what her body produces is waste does not experience this as a teaching she received. She experiences it as something she knows. The distinction is invisible from inside the state. The suggestion has been accepted without evaluation because it arrived before the critical faculty was developed enough to evaluate it — in childhood, through authority figures, through repetition, through the emotional charge of shame and hygiene and the social consequences of being seen as unclean.

Temple notes that children between the ages of zero and seven are in a state of maximum suggestibility — not as a vulnerability unique to them, but as the ordinary mechanism by which culture installs itself. The child's critical faculty has not yet formed. Everything arrives as suggestion and everything is accepted. The vocabulary of the body — what it is, what it produces, what those productions mean — is installed in this window. And what is installed in that window does not present itself, in adult life, as installed. It presents itself as known.

This is the mechanism that erasure required. Not censorship of adult women who already understood what their bodies produced. Not argument with practitioners who held the knowledge intact. Those interventions were necessary for the burning — and the burning happened, in Alexandria, in the witch trials, in the systematic exclusion of women from medical institutions across centuries. But the burning alone cannot maintain an erasure. Fire clears the library. The vocabulary does the rest. Once the word *waste* is installed in the child before she has any framework to question it, the library does not need to be burned again. She will not go looking for what waste is for.

The body was not only removed from inquiry. The language that would have made inquiry feel natural was replaced with language that makes it feel unnecessary — or shameful — or simply unthinkable, the way thoughts about waste are unthinkable.

## The Specific Words

It is worth naming them precisely, because the mechanism only becomes visible when the individual instances are examined.

**Waste.** The word that reclassified the body's most refined endogenous output as the thing the sewage system was built to carry away. A distillation tradition five thousand years old, documented in the oldest myth in the written record, encoded in the apparatus that still bears its inventors' names — collapsed into one syllable. The word arrived in medical education, in public health campaigns, in the hygiene instruction delivered to children as a matter of basic sanitation. It sounded like neutral description. It was classification. And classification determines what questions can be formed about the thing classified.

**Primitive.** The word that reclassified the sacred cow traditions, the panchagavya pharmacy, the Vedic cosmology that understood Soma as the hidden substance of biological intelligence — as the superstition of pre-scientific peoples who did not yet have access to modern understanding. The word performs a temporal hierarchy: what came before is necessarily less sophisticated than what came after. The five thousand years of pharmacological knowledge embedded in the ahimsa traditions is rendered, by this single word, as something already superseded. The critical faculty is not invited to evaluate the tradition on its own terms. The word has already done the evaluation.

**Hygienic.** The word that made the replacement feel like improvement. The douche. The deodorant. The antifungal spray applied to a microbiome that was not malfunctioning. Each one arrived in the language of hygiene — which carried the authority of medical science and the emotional charge of social acceptability. To question the product was to question cleanliness. The bypass was built into the frame. The critical faculty that might have asked *what is this disrupting? what intelligence was here before?* encountered instead the smooth wall of what everyone agreed was simply modern, clean, responsible.

**Inefficient.** The word that reclassified traditional animal husbandry — the keeping of cows not as production units but as the external pharmacy the panchagavya tradition understood them to be — as economically backward. Factory farming is efficient. The sacred cow is inefficient. The vocabulary of productivity determines what practices survive institutional support and what practices are left to die as the communities that held them are urbanised, relocated, their land use restructured. The word does not need to engage with what was lost. Efficiency does not ask what the practice was for.

**Anecdotal.** The word that reclassifies experiential knowledge — the knowledge that lived in the hands of midwives, the knowledge accumulated across generations of women observing their own bodies, the direct phenomenological record of practitioners — as a lower category of evidence, disqualified from serious consideration before it can be evaluated. The word does not engage with the observation. It reclassifies the observer. Once the observer is classified as merely anecdotal, what she observed does not require examination. The question closes before it opens.

## The Body as the Remaining Text

Every one of these words operated on the vocabulary — on the naming system — before it operated on the body itself. The question about the body could not form once the vocabulary prevented it. The woman who has been taught that what she produces is waste will not examine it. The practitioner who has been taught that traditional knowledge is anecdotal will not transmit it. The culture that has absorbed the word *primitive* will not fund the research that might confirm what the tradition already knew.

The mechanism is elegant in its economy. You do not need to control what every woman thinks about her body. You only need to control the vocabulary through which she thinks about it. Install the vocabulary early, through trusted channels, in the high-suggestibility window of childhood and institutional education, and the critical faculty — when it finally develops — will find no language available with which to form the question. The question does not arise because the words that would have constructed it have been replaced with words that make the territory seem already mapped, already classified, already understood.

What the alchemical encoding was protecting against was precisely this. When the plain language of the body became dangerous — when saying *the living waters are medicine* was evidence of witchcraft — the tradition went underground into a vocabulary that sounded like something else. Fire and metals and ascending birds. A language that the institutions could not use against you because it did not say plainly what it was describing. The encoding was a survival strategy. The women who built it understood that the vocabulary was the target.

They were right. It was the vocabulary that fell first.

## Breaking the Trance

Temple identifies one reliable mechanism for exiting the hypnotic state: the critical faculty re-engages when it encounters incongruity. Something that does not fit the installed framework. A fact that the vocabulary cannot absorb without strain. A direct experience that the word cannot adequately describe.

For this body of knowledge, the incongruity is the body itself.

The body keeps producing what it produces regardless of what word has been assigned to it. The living waters do not become waste because a word says so. They carry what they carry — the hormonal signature, the peptide intelligence, the electrochemical record of the body's internal state — whether or not anyone is looking. The cow produces panchagavya in the field whether or not the tradition that understood panchagavya is intact. The amniotic fluid assembles the fetus from itself whether or not anyone in the culture surrounding the birth understands what it is doing.

The laboratory that could not be closed keeps running.

The incongruity arises when a woman looks at what her body has made and the word *waste* does not fit what she is seeing. When the measurement shows what the measurement shows and the vocabulary that was supposed to describe it cannot hold the data. When the body communicates something the installed language has no category for.

That moment of incongruity is the beginning of inquiry. Not the conclusion — the beginning. The critical faculty re-engages not with answers but with the question it was prevented from forming. *What is this? What does it do? What is it for?*

The word was installed before the question. The question comes back from the body.

The body was always the remaining text. The one record that could not be burned because it was not kept in a library. The knowledge encoded in five thousand years of women's practice is not in a book — it is in the biological reality the books were describing. The living waters do not require the library to continue doing what they do. They require only that someone looks without the word already closing their eyes.

This is what the archive is for. Not to replace the vocabulary with a new one that will eventually calcify into the same kind of closure. But to restore the question — to create space between the word and the thing, to let the body be examined before it is named, to trust that what direct observation produces is a more reliable guide to what is happening than any word installed before the observation began.

The word *waste* is a suggestion. It was accepted without evaluation. It can be examined. And in the examination, the question the word was installed to prevent finally gets to form.

*What is this?*

The body answers. It has been answering all along.
