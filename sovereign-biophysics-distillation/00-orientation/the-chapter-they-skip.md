# The Chapter They Skip

*What the Hatha Yoga Pradipika actually says — and what gets taught instead.*

---

The Hatha Yoga Pradipika is not an obscure text. It is the foundational document of hatha yoga, the source material that 200-hour teacher trainings cite, the text that sits on the shelf of every serious yoga practitioner in the world. Chapter 3 is the mudra chapter — khechari, uddiyana, mula bandha, jalandhara. These are taught. These are transmitted. These appear in advanced trainings, in certification programs, in lineage-based schools.

Chapter 3 also contains vajroli mudra, amaroli, and sahajoli.

These are not taught.

---

## What Chapter 3 Actually Contains

The mudra chapter of the HYP moves through a complete sequence. It covers the physical mudras — the bandhas, the locks, the inversions. Then it covers khechari at length: the tongue turned upward to seal the palatal vault, preventing the descent of soma. The HYP spends more verses on khechari than on almost anything else in the text. The instruction is meticulous. The purpose is explicit: to stop the nectar from falling.

The question that follows — where does the nectar go, and what is it — is answered three sections later.

**Vajroli mudra**: the preservation and return of the sexual fluid, drawn back upward through the body's channels rather than lost in expenditure. The text is explicit that the fluid is the foundation of the practice: *"By the preservation of bindu, the body of the Yogi emits a pleasing smell. There is no fear of death so long as the bindu is well established in the body."*

**Amaroli**: immediately following vajroli. The practice of drinking the midstream of the first morning urine. *"That which is called Amar is the old precept handed down in the tantras. The first portion of urine is sharp; the last is useless. The middle portion, called 'Amari', is drunk. The Yogi who practices Vajroli Mudra, drinking Amaroli and anointing his body with its ashes, will attain liberation."*

The word *amara* means immortal. The same root as amrita, as ambrosia, as the deathless nectar. The HYP is not using the word loosely. It is naming the substance.

**Sahajoli**: the companion practice. Mixed ash with amaroli, applied externally. The same substance, the same text, a different preparation.

These three practices — vajroli, amaroli, sahajoli — appear in the same chapter as khechari. They are not in a supplementary text. They are not a later addition. They are in the sequence, verse by verse, in the foundational document of hatha yoga.

## The Pali Canon: Even More Direct

The Theravada Buddhist Vinaya Pitaka — the monastic code, the most conservatively transmitted body of text in the Buddhist world — enumerates the *ājīvikabhesajja*: the lifetime medicines, the small category of substances a fully ordained monk may retain permanently.

Among these: **pūtimuttabhesajja**.

*Pūti*: fermented, bacterially transformed, decomposed. *Mutta*: urine. *Bhesajja*: medicine.

Fermented urine medicine. Not fresh urine — the word *pūti* specifically encodes the transformation that bacterial aging produces. The same process the lunar-cycle protocol produces. The canon names the preparation method in the name of the substance.

This is not peripheral teaching. The medicine section of the Mahavagga is addressed to monks at ordination. The preceptor instructs the newly ordained: *"For the rest of your life you are to endeavour at that."* Fermented urine medicine is one of the four foundational supports of the ordained life — alongside robes, alms food, and shelter at the foot of a tree. Communities that prohibited intoxicants, accumulation of possessions, and sexual contact explicitly named fermented urine as a permanent possession and a lifelong medicine.

Twenty-five centuries of conservative monastic transmission. Unchanged.

## The Advanced Practice Question

The standard response, when the mudra chapter comes up, is that vajroli and amaroli are "advanced practices" — reserved for serious adepts, transmitted through direct lineage, not appropriate for general instruction. This is the frame that allows the practices to remain in the text without being taught.

The question this raises is simple: where are the adepts?

If vajroli and amaroli have been continuously transmitted to a serious few through unbroken lineage — if these practices are genuinely alive in the tradition but withheld from general instruction for good pedagogical reasons — then they exist somewhere. There are teachers who hold them. There are practitioners in whom they are established. There is a living body of knowledge about what these practices do, how they develop, what they produce.

This body of knowledge is not visible in the modern yoga world. The certification bodies don't acknowledge it. The lineage-based schools don't transmit it. The teacher training programs — including the ones that explicitly claim to teach the full HYP — move through the mudra chapter and emerge on the other side having covered khechari but not what follows it.

What is being called "advanced" is a practice that the text presents as sequential and accessible: do vajroli, then do amaroli, then anoint the body with what remains. The complexity is the same order as what surrounds it. The tradition does not describe these as requiring decades of preparation before approach. It describes them as practices.

The "advanced" frame is not a transmission decision. It is a disappearance with a name attached.

---

## What the Texts Have Not Done

The texts have not changed. The Hatha Yoga Pradipika still contains Chapter 3. The Vinaya Pitaka still contains the medicine section of the Mahavagga. The Damar Tantra still opens with what it opens with. The Charaka Samhita still lists *mutra* — urine — among the primary medicines of classical Ayurveda, with an entire section on its therapeutic applications by source and preparation.

None of these texts have revised their content. None have issued corrections. The Theravada monastic communities that preserved the Pali Canon through twenty-five centuries of careful transmission did not remove the passage about fermented urine medicine. They preserved it alongside everything else they considered essential.

What happened is not in the texts. It is in what gets selected from the texts, what gets called advanced and placed out of reach, what gets re-translated as metaphor when the plain meaning is uncomfortable. The clothes, the diet, the breath, the posture, the philosophy — these are held and transmitted. The medicine that the same texts name as the center of the practice is set aside.

The texts are still there. They have been saying the same thing for a very long time.
