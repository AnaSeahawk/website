# All the Practice Is for the Medicine

---

This is the thesis of this archive. Everything else is elaboration.

The Hatha Yoga Pradipika describes khechari mudra — the tongue turned upward to seal the palatal vault — as stopping the soma nectar from falling. The practice exists to catch and preserve a substance. Vajroli mudra preserves the sexual fluid, returning it upward through the body's channels rather than losing it in expenditure. Pranayama builds the internal fire that refines what the kidneys produce. The diet clears and establishes the ground the fire operates on. The microcosmic orbit circulates what is being refined. All of this — every practice in the sequence — is oriented toward a substance the body produces.

That substance is urine.

Not metaphorically. The same HYP chapter that teaches khechari teaches amaroli: the drinking of the midstream of the first morning urine, named *amara* — immortal — in the same family of words as amrita, ambrosia, the deathless nectar. The Pali Canon names fermented urine medicine — *pūtimuttabhesajja* — as one of the four foundational supports of the ordained Buddhist life. The preceptor says at ordination: *"For the rest of your life you are to endeavour at that."* The Damar Tantra, the Charaka Samhita, the Taoist alchemical tradition — each lineage, in its own language, names the same substance as the medicine the practices are designed to produce and refine.

The practices are not ends in themselves. They are not abstract paths to liberation. They are the cultivation of the medicine. The robes, the alms food, the shelter at the foot of the tree — these are the conditions. The medicine is what the conditions are for.

This is what is missing from the modern transmission of these traditions. Not the practices — those are taught, refined, certified, transmitted with great care. The medicine at the center is gone. The practitioner in the temple, the teacher in the lineage school, the yogi working through the HYP chapter by chapter — they are doing the cultivation and discarding the harvest every morning without knowing it is there.

The texts have not changed. The Hatha Yoga Pradipika still says what it says. The Pali Canon still says what it says. The traditions did not revise their content. What changed is what gets selected, what gets called advanced and placed out of reach, what gets translated as metaphor when the plain meaning is uncomfortable.

## They Are Making It

This is the part that is almost impossible to sit with once you see it.

The practice is working. The body does not wait for permission — it produces the medicine every morning regardless of whether the practitioner knows to receive it. The years of pranayama are building the internal fire that refines what the kidneys produce. The khechari practitioner conditioning the tongue to catch the descending nectar from above has no idea that the urine is the other end of the same system. The monk in the robes, eating the alms food, sleeping at the foot of the tree, observing every precept of the ordained life — the body that life is producing is making the fourth foundational support every single day. The vow was taken. The body kept it. The medicine is being poured away.

Every morning. Into the toilet. Down the sewer.

Decades of practice. Years of dietary discipline, breath work, posture, meditation, study, devotion — all of it refining the starting material, making the medicine more potent, more specific, more aligned with the body that produced it. And then it goes into the sewer. And then the practitioner rises, prepares for the morning practice, and does it all again.

The cultivation is not failing. That is what makes this so difficult to look at. The more dedicated the practice, the more refined what is being discarded. The practitioner who has maintained pranayama for twenty years, who has stabilised the diet, who has worked the microcosmic orbit until it runs without deliberate attention — that body is producing something different than the body that began. The still would show it. The practice is doing exactly what the tradition designed it to do.

The question is only whether what the practice produces is being received.

This needs to be said plainly: the nissaya are not advanced practice. They are the opposite. They are the absolute minimum — the irreducible foundation given to every monk at ordination, before any teaching, before any meditation instruction, before any lineage transmission. *Here are the four things you can always fall back on. These are your basics for life.* Rag robes. Alms food. Shelter at the foot of a tree. Fermented urine as medicine. Not chapter three of an esoteric text. Not a secret teaching given after thirty years. The first instruction. The ground. What you cannot be ordained without receiving.

The fourth support is unknown to most of the people who took the vow.

That is not a gap in advanced attainment. That is a gap in the basics. The absolute foundation of the ordained life is missing one of its four legs — and nobody noticed, or noticed and said nothing, or said something and was not heard. The practitioners are not failing at an advanced practice. They are missing something they were given on the first day.

It is also worth noting that this is not limited to the ancient texts. The five-volume *Chinese Medical Qigong Therapy* series — a modern clinical medical textbook used in Chinese Medicine programs — maps the entire relationship explicitly. Volume 2 documents how the internal alchemical sequence (Jing refined into Qi, Qi refined into Shen) is directly shaped by diet, breath, movement, sexual energy cultivation, and meditative practice. The whole series — from energetic anatomy through differential diagnosis to prescription exercises — is a systematic account of how specific practices change what the body's internal alchemical process produces. The medicine is the through-line. The practices are its cultivation.

The sophistication that keeps people from seeing this is not intellectual sophistication. It is the sophistication of the block. It takes real effort — the accumulated effort of years of cultural conditioning — to read *"fermented urine medicine, for the rest of your life"* and not register it. But that effort is invisible. It does not feel like effort. It feels like the natural, obvious, common-sense reading. And the actual plain reading — taking the text at its word, following the logic where it goes — looks radical. Looks fringe. Looks like the kind of thing a serious practitioner would never entertain. The connections are not hidden. The block is what makes them invisible.

This is not a criticism of the practitioners. They are working with the transmission they were given, and the transmission they were given had a gap in it. The gap is not their fault. But it is time to name it, because the texts are unambiguous, the instruction is intact, and the medicine is being made right now in every body that has committed to this practice — and in most of those bodies, it is going down the drain every morning while the practitioner prepares for another session of the cultivation that produced it.

This archive takes the texts at their word.
