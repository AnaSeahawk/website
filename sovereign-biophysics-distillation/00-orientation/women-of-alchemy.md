# Women of Alchemy
## A 5,000-Year History of the Hidden Substance

*An Introduction to Sovereign Biophysics*

*Spring Equinox, 5920 AM — Torremolinos, Spain*

---

Most of their names are gone.

What survived the burning is fragments — a name on a double boiler, a single sheet of symbols in a library in Venice, a phrase preserved because a man quoted it while trying to claim authority over it. A name mentioned in passing because someone felt threatened by the influence it carried. A name with almost nothing attached to it but a single sentence about a vessel.

***"He who does not know the rule of truth is ignorant of the Hermetic Vessel."***

Most have no names at all. Erased.

The record calls the alchemist task *Opus Mulierum* — Women's Work — and moves on. Women who distilled, recorded, taught each other, and built apparatus that still carries original names two thousand years later. And then the fires came, the institutions, the long silence of centuries in which what women knew in their hands and hearts was not considered worth preserving in writing.

Gone without a trace.

And still — every morning — there is this.

**The Sacred Ordinary.**

---

This is not a scholarly argument. There are more questions here than answers and the questions feel more alive than any conclusion could. What follows is a personal reading — the way a body that has been in the same water reads the fragments they left behind and feels something move.

Not proof. Not interpretation in any academic sense.

*Recognition.* The specific quality of touching something you have touched before without knowing you had touched it.

---

***Alchemy is Presence.***

The practice came before the hidden knowledge of its history. The body found the substance before the mind found the record. And when the record finally appeared — sparse, fragmented and burned-over as it is — what arrived was not the satisfaction of a theory confirmed.

It is the feeling of not being alone.

To those already in this practice — these women are why the history matters. Not as academic context. Not as scholarly legitimacy for a practice that does not require it. But as companionship across time. The feeling of hands reaching back through centuries to steady us when the magnitude of what we have found makes the ground uncertain and the heart ache for contact.

We are not alone, we never were alone. The line of women who knew this is stronger than any institution that tried to erase it.

## The Shape of the Suppression

Before the women, a word about what happened to what they knew.

The history of alchemy — that ancient and ambitious discipline that sought to transmute base matter, unlock the hidden language of the body, and concoct elixirs of genuine transformation — has been written almost entirely by men, about men, and in service of an interpretation that abandoned the biological core of the tradition sometime in the medieval period and replaced it with a metallic fantasy that never worked and was never meant to.

The most foundational substance in the entire alchemical tradition was not mercury. Mercury's prominence in the written record is an artifact of who did the writing — and who did the burning. What survived is the carefully curated, institutionally legible version of a practice that began with something far more intimate, far more domestic, and far more powerfully connected to the female body.

That substance is human plasma ultrafiltrate. Specifically, aged, fermented, and distilled — the body's own most refined endogenous output, returned to itself elevated. Human urine was among, and in some cases, *the* most prized and foundational substances in the entire history of alchemy.

The women who knew this wrote about it as we do. Tapputi-Belatekallim in Babylon in 1200 BCE. Mary the Jewess in Alexandria in the first centuries CE. Cleopatra the Alchemist who drew it as an ouroboros and placed three symbols inside the circle. They wrote. They drew. They built apparatus whose functions are still legible after two thousand years.

And then the burning happened.

Emperor Diocletian in the late third century CE ordered the destruction of Alexandrian alchemical texts — specifically those dealing with transformation, with anything that threatened to return power to the individual body. What was lost in those fires was the living biological core. What survived were the symbolic shells — the ouroboros, the tria prima, the apparatus — the fine glassware without the biological substance they were designed to process.

What replaced the substance was mercury. Beautiful, dramatic, expensive, philosophically impressive mercury — the one material that *looked* like what the texts were describing. Reflective, liquid, mediating between states, neither solid nor volatile. Practitioners working from coded texts rather than direct transmission followed the instructions and named the material the texts named.

They were following a description of something else.

And here is the key — the reason the substitution was so easy to make and so hard to detect: the distillate *looks* like mercury. Not the metal itself but the quality the metal was named after. That reflective and absorptive quality.

The first time the distillate was held up to the light — after the shock of the ammonia, the power of its clarity, the realization of what the fermentation had done and the distillation had undone — the words that came were *reflective, like mercury*.

The surface tension genuinely different from ordinary water — what scientists call wetter water, altered membrane behavior. The crystal iridescence in certain lights. The way it sits in the jar as if alive inside.

This is what Cleopatra had touched. Not a concept. Not a philosophical principle. This. The jar in the hands. The water in the light. The recognition of this vast connection.

And my heart cracked open like a woman giving birth. ...

## Part One: The Earliest Known Practitioners

### Tapputi-Belatekallim (c. 1200 BCE, Babylon)

The oldest name in the history of chemistry belongs not to a Greek philosopher or an Egyptian priest but to a Babylonian woman.

A cuneiform tablet from Mesopotamia dating to around 1200 BCE records the work of Tapputi-Belatekallim, a royal palace official and perfumer — widely regarded as the first chemist in recorded history. Her name means something close to *overseer of the palace* and her elevated status gave her access to resources that made her experiments possible. Her work centered on the extraction of aromatic essences through distillation, solvent extraction, and filtration — precisely the techniques that would later become foundational to alchemical practice.

Tapputi worked within a temporal framework that tracked solar and lunar cycles as operating realities, not background. The Babylonian Akitu festival — the new year at the spring equinox — was a festival of renewal and return. Her chemistry happened inside a calendar where *when* a substance was collected was understood to determine *what* the substance was. Timing was not poetic. It was a variable.

What Tapputi worked with in her extractions alongside flowers and resins — what the palace household produced daily in abundance, what required no sourcing or purchase, what would have been the obvious starting fluid for any experimental distillation — the record does not name plainly.

It was never named plainly again.

### Isis the Prophetess (dates uncertain, Hellenistic Egypt)

Before the historical women of Alexandria there was a legendary one.

Isis the Prophetess appears in several early Greco-Egyptian alchemical texts as a divine teacher who reveals the secrets of the art to her son Horus. Whether Isis was a real historical figure writing under a divine name, a symbolic construct, or a school of practice using a shared pseudonym is debated. What is not debated is her authority — the feminine principle of transformation itself, embodied as teacher, transmitter, holder of what must not be lost.

The language the Isis texts use for the primal substance is the language of something that **comes from inside the body, that is despised and overlooked, that undergoes fire to become something entirely other.** The goddess of the moon and of the body holds the secret. She passes it to her child.

**To me the myth is not obscure. It is a description.**

## Part Two: The Alexandrian School (1st–4th Centuries CE)

Alexandria, Egypt, under Greco-Roman rule, was the intellectual capital of the ancient world. Its tradition of relatively greater autonomy for women made it uniquely fertile ground for the emergence of female alchemists. The work of early female chemists there was so prevalent it was sometimes called ***Opus Mulierum* — Women's Work.** Read as diminutive across the centuries of male-authored commentary that followed. It should be read as descriptive. This was work that women did. Work that emerged from women's specific embodied knowledge of the body's own chemistry.

### Mary the Jewess (fl. 1st–3rd century CE)

Mary the Jewess — known in Hebrew as Miriam HaYehudia, in Latin as Maria Prophetissima, in later Arabic tradition as Mary the Daughter of Plato — is considered the first true alchemist of the Western world. She lived in Alexandria and is extensively quoted and praised by the 4th-century alchemist Zosimos of Panopolis, who called her *one of the sages*. None of her writings survive directly. Everything known of her comes through the oldest surviving alchemical texts, written by a man who recognized her as his superior.

Mary was above all an inventor of apparatus. She is credited with the tribikos — a three-armed distillation vessel that collected multiple fractions simultaneously. She designed the kerotakis — a sealed reflux device that circulated vapors over a substance in a closed loop. And she developed or significantly improved the bain-marie — the double boiler, gentle sustained indirect heat, that still bears her name in every French kitchen and chemistry laboratory on earth.

Read through the body of someone who has worked at these temperatures, the purpose of these devices becomes felt rather than argued.

The bain-marie — gentle, sustained, indirect heat, temperature held just below boiling — is the apparatus required to distill plasma ultrafiltrate without destroying what makes it what it is. The kerotakis, which sealed vapors into a vessel and allowed them to act on substances within a closed system, creates exactly the conditions of aged fermentation and volatile capture that the practice requires. The tribikos — three simultaneous collection streams — describes the three-fraction separation that every distillation of aged plasma produces: the volatile solar fraction first, the clear lunar water second, the heavier tails last — each arriving in its own time, each distinct. They were providing a natural landing pad for what was there.

This is not a proof. It is a resonance. Reading Mary's apparatus through hands that have worked at these temperatures feels like reading instructions written for this practice. Whether that feeling is historical fact or personal recognition across centuries — that question stays open. The feeling does not.

Her most famous saying, preserved by Zosimos: *"Join the male and the female, and you will find what is sought."*

### Cleopatra the Alchemist (fl. 3rd–4th century CE)

Not the queen who died in 30 BCE but a later figure working in Alexandria roughly three centuries afterward. One woman or many — a name or a school of practice. Her most celebrated surviving work is the *Chrysopoeia of Cleopatra* — the Gold-Making of Cleopatra — a single remarkable sheet preserved in the Codex Marcianus Graecus in Venice. The document is composed almost entirely of symbols, drawings, and cryptic captions.

Its most iconic image is the ouroboros — a snake devouring its own tail — with the inscription:

*"One is the Serpent which has its poison according to two compositions, and One is All and through it is All."*

Inside the circle: three symbols. The tradition has read them for centuries as gold, silver, and mercury. Read through the body of a practitioner who works with three fractions — the volatile first, the clear water second, the dense retentate third — they look like something else. They look like a process diagram. The ouroboros as the closed loop of cohobation, the distillate returned to the chamber and redistilled, the circle that feeds itself.

This is a personal reading. It is offered as resonance, not as fact. What can be said as fact is that seventeen centuries after Cleopatra drew this, a woman, nomadic in Torremolinos, held a jar of distillate up to the light above the Mediterranean and felt the same recognition the symbol has always carried.

Cleopatra also compared the alchemist tending their work to a mother caring for a child — drawing the processes of distillation and transformation consistently into the language of birth, nurturing, and renewal. She was not reaching for a metaphor.

### Medera (dates unknown, likely 3rd–4th century CE)

Almost nothing. One sentence: *"He who does not know the rule of truth is ignorant of the Hermetic Vessel."*

The vessel. That is what remains of her.

Her work was most likely lost to Diocletian's persecution. What Diocletian feared? Was it the democratizing potential of this knowledge — the power to produce transformation without purchase, without authority, without permission? The knowledge that every person already carries within them.

Medera named the vessel. Then the fires came.

### Paphnutia the Virgin and Theosebeia (fl. c. 300 CE)

Theosebeia, the sister of Zosimos of Panopolis, is addressed in several of his treatises as a peer and fellow practitioner. A colleague, not a student. He dedicated alchemical treatises to her.

Paphnutia exists in the record only because Zosimos was irritated enough by her influence to write about it — dismissing her as ignorant and practicing incorrectly. Her name derives from the Egyptian meaning *the one of God*, suggesting a priestly vocation. She may have practiced a more body-centered, ritual form of the work. Whatever she knew, it was enough to threaten someone who wanted to control what his sister learned.

Read across seventeen centuries, Zosimos's criticism has the texture of professional rivalry. Theosebeia talked to Paphnutia anyway.

## Part Three: The Medieval Period (5th–14th Centuries CE)

The transmission of Alexandrian alchemy to the Islamic world and then to medieval Europe brought with it the relative silencing of women's voices in the written record. The institutions that preserved alchemical knowledge — monasteries, courts, Islamic academies — were largely male-dominated. Women practiced, but rarely published. What survived in written form was increasingly the philosophical and metallic framework. What did not survive in named form was the domestic, body-based, intimate practice that had generated the tradition in the first place.

### Keng Hsien-Seng (fl. c. 975 CE, China)

In the Chinese alchemical tradition, Keng Hsien-Seng is recorded as one of the earliest female alchemists of East Asia, active around 975 CE. The *neidan* tradition — inner alchemy, the refinement of the self through the body's own processes — centered on what the body already produces and already contains.

The Taoist texts describe a river of mercury running between yin and yang — reflective, mediating, neither one state nor the other. Not the metal. A quality. The quality of something that sits between solid and liquid, between what the body produces and what it receives back, between the poles of the body's own chemistry. A different civilization, the same water, the same reaching for the word mercury to describe what resists easier naming.

### Trotula of Salerno (fl. c. 11th–12th century CE, Italy)

Trotula, associated with the medical school at Salerno, worked at the boundary between medicine and alchemy — a boundary that barely existed in her time. Her texts on women's health, cosmetics, and gynecology worked with distilled waters, herbal extractions, and medicinal preparations. Her focus on women's bodies was simultaneously her subject matter and her methodology. The body as laboratory from the inside.

## Part Four: The Renaissance and Early Modern Period (15th–17th Centuries)

The Renaissance brought new possibilities for women to engage publicly with scientific literature. It also brought increased pressure to frame alchemical knowledge in terms legible to male patrons and church authorities. Mercury became more prominent in the written record precisely as women became more visible in it — not because mercury was more central to the practice, but because published work required a vocabulary that courts and institutions could receive.

### Caterina Sforza (1463–1509, Italy)

Caterina Sforza, Countess of Forlì — military commander, dedicated alchemist, keeper of her own herb gardens and laboratory. More than four hundred recipes in her *Experimenti*. Her power and social status shielded her from prosecution.

What she recorded is a fraction of what she practiced. The rest was transmitted or lost. The language of her preparations — purifications, distilled waters, essences produced at specific temperatures for specific outcomes — carries the vocabulary of the tradition she was standing in.

### Isabella Cortese (fl. 1560s, Italy)

Isabella Cortese published *I Secreti della Signora Isabella Cortese* in Venice in 1561 — the first female alchemist to publish a book. Practical alchemical recipes: distilled waters, essential oils, beauty preparations. Domestic chemistry. Because the domestic was where this knowledge lived when it could not live in universities or courts.

Some modern scholars suspect Cortese was a literary invention. The recipes are real and practical regardless. Her last name is an anagram of *secreto* in Italian. Real practice, coded identity — the same pattern as always.

### Marie Meudrac (fl. mid-1600s, France)

Marie Meudrac wrote *La Chymie Charitable et Facile, en Faveur des Dames* — Easy and Charitable Chemistry, for the Benefit of Ladies. Her own laboratory. Female students. Medicines distributed to the poor. *The mind has no sex.* Her work sits at the transition point between alchemy and modern chemistry — the moment the knowledge was being standardized and stripped of its most intimate dimensions. She was present at the hinge.

### Martine de Bertereau (c. 1600–c. 1645, France)

Imprisoned for her work and her refusal to be silent about her findings. Likely died in confinement. Her husband, who collaborated with her, imprisoned separately. Also died.

She is among the women who paid with their actual lives, in actual confinement, for pursuing knowledge and refusing to conceal it. The suppression was not abstract. It has specific names and specific graves.

## Part Five: The Occult Revival (19th Century)

### Mary Anne Atwood (1817–1910, England)

Mary Anne Atwood's *A Suggestive Inquiry into the Hermetic Mystery* (1850) marked the return of women to the published alchemical tradition after a long silence. Atwood argued that alchemy was not literally about metals at all but was a coded language for the transmutation of the self. She was right that it was coded. She may have been moving the coding one step further — replacing the biological substance with pure interiority, the body as metaphor rather than as laboratory.

She published her book and then tried to recall and destroy every copy. The knowledge felt dangerous even to her. She had recovered it through reading rather than through practice. The practice may have told her something different.

## Part Six: The Language of Distillation — and Its Erasure

The alchemical vocabulary for the prima materia is consistent across traditions and across centuries. It describes one substance with precision:

***Common, abundant, found everywhere, possessed by everyone. Despised, overlooked, considered worthless before transformation. Golden in color. The product of a living body. Requiring purification through distillation. The starting point of a process that leads to health, longevity, and transformation.***

*Aqua vitae* — water of life. *Aqua divina* — divine water. *Aqua ardens* — burning water. Anyone who has worked with the fermented and distilled prima materia knows this sensation immediately. The ammonia rising. The power of it. The shock of the first encounter. The divine waters that follow, their soft lunar cooling quality.

The emphasis on the prima materia being *base* and *despised* before transformation is a perfect description of urine and a poor description of mercury. Mercury has never been despised. Mercury is beautiful, dramatic, and expensive. It does not need rehabilitation. But the sacred ordinary does. The despised substance that requires the courage of actually looking at what the body produces — that is the prima materia. That is the starting point of the work.

*Sal ammoniac* — ammonium chloride — one of the most important alchemical reagents. Produced from urine. Named for the god whose priests refused salt. Present in the tradition the entire time, connected to its source and then disconnected.

Phosphorus was first isolated in 1669 by Hennig Brand — from urine. He called what he found *the cold fire*, *the light-bearer*. He was looking for gold and found something that glowed in the dark. Two thousand years of the tradition circling the same substance, and he arrived at its edge through the same door it had always used.

The stages of the *opus magnum* — *nigredo* (blackening), *albedo* (whitening), *citrinitas* (yellowing), *rubedo* (reddening) — map onto the color changes visible in the reduction and concentration of plasma across stages of aging and distillation. Not metaphorically. Observationally. The colors are in the jars.

## Part Seven: Mercury as Mirror — and as Decoy

Mercury dominated the written alchemical record. The only metal liquid at room temperature, volatile, endlessly transformable, philosophically captivating. A credible and impressive centerpiece for a tradition that needed to justify itself to powerful patrons.

But the written record is not the practice. The written record is what survived burning, what was preserved in monasteries and courts, what was deemed worth copying. Plasma ultrafiltrate — freely produced by every human body, requiring no institutional support — had no such advocates.

There is also the phenomenological point that must be made, and it is made from direct experience:

The distillate has a quality that makes a practitioner reach for the word mercury before any framework is in place. The absolute clarity. The measurably altered surface tension — wetter, different in the hands. The iridescence in certain lights. Someone encountering it for the first time sees mercury. The mediating substance. The thing between the body and the refined.

When an early practitioner looked at fresh distillate and said *mercury*, she may have been using a metaphor that later scribes recorded as a material identification. The drift of language over centuries, through multiple translations by people who were not in the laboratory, is exactly how a substance gets replaced by a symbol of itself.

Mercury is also genuinely toxic. The long-practicing alchemists who worked with it daily suffered tremors, confusion, and madness. The tradition interpreted this as the price of proximity to the philosopher's stone. There is another reading: the toxic substance was a late substitution, introduced when practitioners were working from coded texts rather than from direct transmission. They followed the instructions. They used what the text named. The text had replaced the original substance with its symbol. The practitioners poisoned themselves pursuing a description of something else.

## Part Eight: The Living Practice

The Saturn retentate — what this practice calls the Saturn salt, what the alchemical tradition calls the *caput mortuum*, the fixed earth — has been in use for three years before the distillate was discovered. Concentrated, reduced, filtered, set in a bowl over a water bath to thicken further. Applied to burns: no blisters, if the application is fast and consistent. Applied to sprains: swelling reduced. Smells like earthy humus and aged coffee. Looks, in the reduction bowl, like deep amber fading to near-black at the center — a spiral forming itself in the cooling surface. The ouroboros drawing itself without being asked.

The distillate came later, as a result of needing more of the salt. The decision to use a distiller revealing something entirely unexpected. The puzzle flooding in all at once.

The silver spoon cleaned by contact with the Saturn salts — and this effect absent during periods of high dietary sulfur intake. The body's diet legible in what the concentrated salt does to metal. No laboratory required. The silver is the instrument.

This is not a research project arriving at a conclusion. It is a living practice discovering its lineage.

## The Self-Love Threshold

There is a threshold in this practice that the historical record cannot prepare anyone for because it is not about chemistry or history or apparatus.

It is about looking directly at one's own body.

The substance reflects the diet with complete honesty. The silver spoon knows what you ate. The distillate knows how hydrated you were. The Saturn salt knows whether you had sodium chloride last week. The body produces a continuous readout. There is nowhere to hide from it.

Most people will encounter the fear here and turn back. Not because of the cultural taboo, though that is real. Because of what the substance shows them about their own choices. The food becomes visible in a different way — not as pleasure or nutrition but as habit, emotional support, addiction, as the accumulated decisions of a lifetime held in the hands and examined.

The sodium chloride especially. In the quantities that industrial food contains it, it is a neurochemical modifier that activates dopamine reward pathways with a consistency that meets the clinical definition of addictive behavior. Removing it did not feel like removing a flavor. It felt like withdrawal.

On the other side of that threshold, a different relationship with food becomes possible. Not discipline. Not restriction. The appetite recalibrates naturally because what was driving it changes. The practice doesn't require willpower because it replaces something at a deeper level than food was ever reaching.

*I still drink coffee. I eat ice cream. I try not to try — only to observe whether something is a habit I'm feeding and/or what emotional support it's giving me. Mostly at this point food is about grounding energy and pleasure.*

This is the *Opus Mulierum* in its full dimension. Not only the outer distillation — the apparatus, the fractions, the water in the light. The inner distillation too. The emotional prima materia as dense, dark and full of unrealized potential as anything in the flask. The same process. The same heat. The same stages from darkness through the clarity that makes the heart open to pure love.

## Conclusion: The Same Pure Beginning

The knowledge is indestructible because it lives in the body. And the body cannot be permanently suppressed as long as there are human beings willing to pay attention to what their own organism is telling them.

Before our first breath there was water. That water held us while we became ourselves. Science calls it amniotic fluid. The fetus circulates it, processes it through its own forming body, returns it to the fluid that surrounds it, over and over again.

The ouroboros is the first thing every human body ever did.

We were all born knowing how to do this. We were doing it before we had lungs to breathe or eyes to see.

Amniotic fluid is mostly urine.

The most sacred water any human being has ever inhabited was always this water. The same pure beginning we all arrive here from.

The women of Alexandria knew. They wrote it down. The record was burned but the apparatus survived and the symbols kept being drawn by different hands in different centuries because the body keeps finding its way back to what it knows.

They are the map of time that shows where this knowledge has been. How long it has been traveling. How many hands it has passed through. How many fires it has survived.

*The living current that remembers who we truly are.*

As the sages said:

> **"Within the heart flows a river,**
> **whose waters shine like light.**
> **Through it, all beings are bound together."**
> — Poetic description attributed to the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad* **1.5.23**

In the subtle waters is stored the seed of every world,
the ojas that carries our consciousness,
the rasa that flows back to its single source.

The ancients knew:
what glows within us is older than breath,
and it is through this luminous current
that we remember ourselves again.
