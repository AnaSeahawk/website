# Where to Begin

---

This archive documents a practice.

The practice is old — older than any written record of it, though the written records go back five thousand years and are surprisingly specific. It involves the body's own living waters: what the body produces, what those productions carry, and what becomes possible when a practitioner works with them directly rather than discarding them.

The short version: the body is a distillery. What moves through it is not waste. The traditions that understood this — Sumerian, Vedic, Egyptian, Alexandrian — left records in myth, in apparatus, in pharmacological systems still in use today. Those records were suppressed, burned, or reclassified as primitive. The knowledge survived in the body itself, because the body cannot be burned.

This archive is the record of one practitioner working her way back through that suppression — through the history, the biology, the lived practice, and the measurements — to document what the body already knows and what the tradition was always describing.

---

## What You Will Find Here

**The lineage** — the women who carried this knowledge through five thousand years, what was done to what they knew, and why it matters that their names are still recoverable.

**The mechanism of erasure** — how a word replaces a question, and which specific words were used to close inquiry into the body's own productions.

**The mythological layer** — how the oldest recorded myth, the story of Inanna, is also a precise description of the alchemical process and the body's own chemistry.

**The external practice** — the sacred cow, panchagavya, the external pharmacy that runs alongside the internal one, and why the traditions that understood both produced the pharmacological systems that still work.

**The empirical record** — one distillation run, measured with modern instruments, documented in full.

**A year of observation** — what the practice looks like across a complete solar cycle, and how it connects to the body's own seasonal and monthly rhythms.

---

## How to Read It

Start at the beginning and follow the chain. Each piece prepares the ground for the next.

The history comes first — not as academic context but because the suppression is part of the practice. You cannot fully receive what the body produces without understanding what was done to the language that would have let you name it. The myth comes after the history, and lands differently once you know what the myth survived. The empirical record comes last in the orientation, because by then you have the framework to understand what the measurements are measuring.

The reading path is marked at the bottom of each piece.

## Who This Is For

Anyone who has felt something stirring in this territory and had no language for it.

Practitioners who are already in the work and want the historical and philosophical depth behind what they are doing.

Those who find the body's own intelligence more compelling than what institutions have said about it.

This is not a course. There is no curriculum, no programme, no outcome promised. It is a living archive — one practitioner's record, shared because the knowledge gains value by moving.
