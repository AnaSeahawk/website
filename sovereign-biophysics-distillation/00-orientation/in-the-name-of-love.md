# In the Name of Love

---

There is a wound at the centre of this work that is harder to name than anything in the historical record.

Not because it is unspeakable. Because naming it implicates the people who loved you most.

The practices documented in the previous piece — foot binding, leblouh, chhaupadi, the cutting, the hygiene campaigns, the centuries of exclusion — all of them arrived in the same register. Not as cruelty. As love. As preparation. As the thing a mother does because she wants her daughter to be safe in a world that has made its requirements clear. The harm was real. The love was also real. Both things were true simultaneously. And that simultaneity is the specific trap — the place where the wound cannot be approached, because approaching it means holding the person who administered it as someone who caused harm, and that person was also the person who loved you most completely.

This is not a peripheral difficulty. It is the architecture of the cycle's persistence.

---

## What Love Conceals

Fear, we said, is the transmission vehicle. Fear for the daughter in the world as it is. That is true, and it is important.

But fear travels inside love. It arrives in the hands of the person who most wants to protect you. And when harm arrives that way — not as attack, not as neglect, not as the absence of care, but as its most concentrated expression — it cannot be named from inside it. The body that receives harm in love does not experience an enemy. It experiences the world. It learns: this is what care feels like. This is what the body requires. This is how things are.

Love is not the mechanism. Love is the concealment. The mechanism is fear, and the architecture is institutional, and the hands that administer it belong to someone who genuinely does not want to hurt you. The love is real. That is what makes it work. A wound delivered by an enemy can be named as harm and assigned its correct address. A wound delivered by love has no address outside the love itself. To name it as harm is to name the love as harm. Most people cannot do that. Most people will not. And so the wound remains unnamed, carried forward, administered again in the next generation in the same hands.

That is what love was used for. Not intentionally. The concealment did not require intention. It required only that the love be real — and it was.

---

## The Backward-Running Force

*Beauty and the Binding* names why the cycle began and why it ran while the external conditions required it. There is another force that keeps it running after those conditions have shifted.

Once a mother has administered the harm, the daughter's refusal — when she is old enough to have a daughter of her own — does not leave the mother's past intact. It retroactively reclassifies it. If the daughter can refuse, and her child is fine, then what was done was not necessary. The fear was real but the wound it demanded was not required. The mother is now someone who caused harm in the name of love — not as a necessary harm, but as a pointless one. Not as the mother who had no choice, but as the mother who had a choice and did not see it.

That reclassification is unbearable. Not because the mother is cruel or indifferent. Because she is a person who loves her daughter, who did what she believed was required, who absorbed the same wound herself and survived it and built a life inside it. To look at it clearly is to look at everything clearly — the grandmother, the great-grandmother, the whole chain of hands that did what the consensus required and called it love. The longer the chain, the more impossible the seeing becomes. At a certain depth, no one takes responsibility because the required admission is no longer personal. It has become generational. It has become the cost of holding the family together.

*You have to do what I did* is a petition. Keep me inside the category of the mother who had no choice. Let the continuation be the proof that there was no other way. Do not make me the person who hurt you for nothing.

The daughter who refuses is not simply making a different choice. She is withdrawing her body from the proof.

The emotion that enforces this on both sides is shame. Not guilt — guilt says *I did something wrong*. Shame says *I am wrong*. Shame says: if this is seen clearly, I will lose the love that makes belonging possible. The question underneath every silence in this territory is *will they still love me* — and it runs in both directions at once. The one who administered the harm asking it of the one who received it. The one who received it asking it of the one who administered it. The one who breaks the chain asking it of everyone. All of them held in place by the threat of the same answer.

The punishment that enforces the silence is withdrawal of love and belonging. This is the lever. It works because love was the original delivery vehicle — the harm and the love arrived together, before the body had any capacity to separate them. Love as the weapon that keeps its own wound in place.

---

## The Same Trap in Every Room

The mother. The doctor. The teacher. The institution.

The authority figure who told you what your body is and what it requires — who defined its productions as waste, its instincts as dangerous, its autonomous knowledge as something to be managed rather than consulted — is also caught in this. Not differently caught. The same trap, the same mechanism.

The doctor who recommended a procedure for thirty years, who told a hundred mothers that their daughters needed something done to them, faces the same retroactive indictment the mother faces. Abandoning the practice is not only a scientific update. It is a self-confrontation. It requires becoming, overnight, the person who caused harm in the name of medicine — who administered the wound in the name of care. The institution will not do this willingly. It will find reasons. It will commission studies. It will move slowly, protect its members, protect its past. Not from malice. From the same unbearable mathematics that keeps the mother silent.

This is why information alone does not break the cycle. The cycle is not primarily maintained by ignorance. It is maintained by the cost of what seeing would require.

---

## What Was Installed Before Language

The practices are the visible form. What they produce is less visible.

A body that receives, before it has language, the message that its own productions are disgusting — does not learn this as a belief. It learns it as a flinch. The flinch arrives first. The thought comes later to explain the flinch, to justify it, to make it feel like reason rather than inheritance. By the time the person is old enough to examine what they believe about their own body, they are examining the thought — and the flinch that preceded it is invisible. It does not present as trauma. It presents as common sense.

This is what generations of this produces: not a population with incorrect beliefs about the body, but a population whose bodies have absorbed a relationship to themselves that precedes and underlies any belief. The body that does not trust its own knowledge. The body that flinches from its own productions. The body that has been separated — quietly, in love, before language — from its own interior authority. That body does not know it lost something. The loss was installed before the thing that was lost could be named.

Body autonomy is spoken of as a political concept. It is that. It is also something more primary and more intimate: the specific sense that your body's knowledge is yours. That what it produces belongs to it. That you are the authority on your own interior — not the institution, not the consensus, not the hands that shaped you before you could refuse. That sense was what was quietly taken. Not through force, mostly. Through love. Through repetition. Through the most complete delivery mechanism there is: the hands of someone who wanted to protect you from what the world would do if you arrived in it unmanaged.

---

## Why It Cannot Be Named

It cannot be named because naming it means naming the people you love.

It cannot be named because the body that carries it does not experience a wound — it experiences itself. The wound has become the self. The flinch is not felt as flinching. It is felt as disgust, as reason, as the correct response to what the body does. There is no memory of a different relationship, because the relationship was altered before memory.

It cannot be named because every authority in your life — the mother, the doctor, the institution, the teacher — is also inside this, also defending their own past, also unable to look directly at what continuation has required of them. To name it is to be alone with it, because the naming has nowhere to land that will be received without costing someone something unbearable.

And it cannot be named because the concealment is love. Real love. The love that bound the feet and the love that held the daughter after were not different loves. They were the same love, carrying what the world had put into it. To separate what the love was carrying from the love itself is the specific work that the cycle makes nearly impossible — because the binding said: *this is what love does.* And the body believed it. Because who knows better than love what love is?

## What We Are Living Now

This is not only history.

The same pattern is running in the present tense, in contemporary forms. The injection the doctor prescribed. The procedure performed on an infant in the first days of life. The pill. The formula when the body could nurse. The vaccines consented to without full understanding of what was being consented to. The menstrual cycle managed into pharmaceutical silence. Not all of these are equivalent in kind or degree. But they share a structure: an authority, an intervention on a body that cannot yet refuse or does not know it can, a framing that calls it care, a consensus that makes refusal feel like abandonment.

And when the consensus begins to shift — when one person begins to question what was done, what was consented to, what may continue into the next generation — the same mathematics runs. The shame. The *will they still love me*. The silence where a conversation should be.

Families are fracturing over these questions right now, in kitchens and in phone calls that don't happen, in the space between parents and adult children, in things no one has found a way to say. A parent who consented to something she later came to question cannot find the words to say *I may have been wrong* — because those words also say *I may have been wrong about you*, about what was done to you, about whether it was necessary. A child who received something and cannot name it as harm without naming the person who loved them most as its instrument. The fracture between them is real. The silence is real. And most people living this are doing so without language for what produced it.

Marshall Rosenberg's work — *Nonviolent Communication: A Language of Life*, a title that undersells it — approaches the same wound from the direction of human needs rather than history. His core observation: every human behavior is an attempt to meet a need, and the deepest needs are love and belonging. Withdrawal of love is the most fundamental punishment precisely because it threatens those needs directly. What he names as the internalized voice of punishment is what this piece calls shame: the threat that became internal, that enforces silence without requiring anyone to threaten anything anymore. His work is a resource for the relational present — for the people trying to find a way across the fracture.

What we are living now is not one family's story. It is something happening at scale, in millions of families, across every culture that is currently renegotiating what was administered in love and what it cost. The renegotiation is happening faster than the language to hold it. The wound is old. The fracture is new. The connection between them — why the contemporary argument over a procedure, a prescription, a decision made in the first weeks of a child's life carries so much heat, so much silence, so much grief that cannot find its address — is what this piece is attempting to name.

---

## What the Archive Is Doing

This archive is not an accusation.

The women who administered these practices — and the women who are still administering them, in the barn at night, in the kitchen, in the doctor's office — are not the architects of the suppression. They inherited it. They were shaped by it before they had any tools to examine it. They administered it in love, which was real, to daughters they wanted to protect, which was real, from a world that had made its requirements clear, which was also real. The love does not make the harm less. The harm does not make the love less. Both are true.

What the archive is doing is naming. Not healing. Not therapy. Not instruction. Documentation of what the body found when it began to recover the relationship that the binding tried to prevent. And in that documentation, the naming of what was done to prevent that recovery — including the most complete mechanism, the one that required no external enforcement, the one that arrived in love.

To name what love was carrying is not to destroy the love. It is to finally hold it accurately. To stop asking love to do the impossible work of making the harm not have happened. To see the mother as someone who was also inside this — also shaped before she had language, also flinching from herself, also doing what she believed was required.

The cycle does not break through accusation. It does not break through argument. It breaks when one person can see clearly enough to hold both things at once: the love was real, and what it was carrying was harm, and neither of those facts cancels the other. That seeing is not comfortable. It is not tidy. It does not produce a villain or a resolution.

It produces, for the first time, the possibility of a different relationship to what the body already knows.
