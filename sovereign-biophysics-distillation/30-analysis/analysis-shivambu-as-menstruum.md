# Shivambu as Menstruum
## Historical Grounding for Herbal Extraction and Cohobation

*A working research entry. Technical details on aromatic herb cohobation protocol in separate laboratory notes.*

---

The idea of using shivambu as the solvent base for herbal preparations, followed by distillation of the combined material, is not a modern innovation. It sits within a documented tradition spanning at least two distinct lineages — the Indic Tantric-Ayurvedic corpus and the European alchemical-spagyric tradition — with additional traces in folk pharmaceutical practice. What has not previously been assembled is the argument that these lineages share a common technical logic, and that the distilled fraction used as the second-pass menstruum represents a natural refinement of principles already implicit in both.

---

## The Indic Lineage: Urine as the Medium, Not the Supplement

The *Damar Tantra*'s Shivambu Kalpa Vidhi contains explicit formulations in which shivambu is combined with specific plant preparations — pepper, *Terminalia belerica*, and *Terminalia chebula* (Triphala's components) taken with shivambu to acquire what the text calls "divine lustre and brightness"; neem leaf juice combined with shivambu to attain yogic states; *Tinospora cordifolia* extract followed by shivambu to "conquer death"; dry ginger powder followed directly by shivambu to relieve disease. What the text encodes here is not casual supplementation. The sequencing — plant preparation *then* shivambu, or plant dissolved *in* shivambu — implies the fluid is acting as the extractive and delivery medium, not merely as a chaser.

The Ayurvedic scriptures more broadly delineate many different formulae for herbal preparations utilising urine as the substrate, described as greatly contributing to the efficacy of both the urine and the herbs. References to urine therapy appear across the full span of the classical Ayurvedic corpus — Sushruta, Harita, Bhavaprakasha, Yogaratnakara, Vagbhata, and Bhaishajya Ratnavali among others — with shivambu classified as *Vishaghna* (destroyer of all poisons) and *Rasayana* (rejuvenating substance capable of reversing ageing).

The critical point is that none of these formulations describe ingestion as the operative mechanism in a modern pharmacological sense. The plant and the fluid are understood as entering into a relationship — the fluid having been processed through a living body, carrying its signature, then receiving the plant's virtue and amplifying or channelling it. This is the operative principle of the menstruum: the solvent is not neutral but active, shaping what it extracts and how that extract functions.

## The European Lineage: Spirit of Urine as Alkahest

In European alchemical practice from the medieval period through the seventeenth century, urine occupied a central and unreduced position. Alchemical texts from ancient China, Greece, Arabia, and Europe call for urine as the *prima materia* or first matter — the standard sign of the Alchemists' *Mercurius philosophorum*, either symbolically or literally in the case of producing solvents, menstruums, and alkahests.

By the Paracelsian period, the distilled spirit of urine had been formally categorised as a menstruum — a prepared solvent for further alchemical work. *Secrets of the Adepts*, a major compilation of alchemical menstruum recipes, explicitly classifies the salt of urine alongside Sal Ammoniac and salt of blood under "Simple Vegetable Menstruums made of Spirit of Philosophical Wine and Volatile Salts" — these are working solvents for plant and mineral extractions, not curiosities.

The technical literature is specific. One documented procedure combines pure spirit of wine with pure spirit of urine, distilling with a very gentle heat, then cohobating the same spirit until all the volatile salt of the urine passes into the spirit of wine — described as "a great dissolvent and alcahest," strengthened by working it again with new spirit of urine as many times as desired. This was called "the wine of the sages." Joannes Agricola's variant uses three successive distillations of urine to half-volume each time, yielding a subtle spirit in which "a beautiful, transparent, shining salt will rise" — this spirit then combined with spirit of wine and distilled to produce a menstruum described as capable of extracting tinctures from metals and minerals.

Robert Boyle refers explicitly to "volatile and urinous spirits" as the category, with the spirit of urine itself named. Jan Baptist van Helmont became well known for producing powerful medicinal preparations he called his *Alkahest* — a universal solvent derived through repeated cohobation of the distillate back over its own caput mortuum. Some historians of alchemy have interpreted this as urine-derived, identifying it with the volatile salt of urine. Van Helmont himself never publicly disclosed the composition; what the alchemical record confirms is the *method* — repeated cohobation — and the *claimed result* — a universal solvent with remarkable capacity to extract virtue from metals and minerals. Whether his specific material was urine-derived remains a matter of historical interpretation, not established fact.

The Paracelsian tradition explicitly categorised vegetable menstruums — solvents for plant extractions — made by combining the spirit of philosophical wine with "the hottest vegetables, herbs, flowers" directly into the menstruum before distillation. The logical extension of combining this with the urinous spirit tradition is exactly what is being proposed here: the distilled shivambu fraction used as the menstruum for plant maceration, then redistilled. The two traditions contain all the components; they simply have not been assembled with the shivambu distillate as the specific base.

## Cohobation: The Named Operation

The technical term for the procedure described — macerating plant material in the distillate and redistilling — is cohobation, and it is orthodox alchemical practice with a long documented history. French alchemical tradition describes the process of putting urine into a sealed vessel, setting it in horse dung for forty days to undergo fermentation and putrefaction, then distilling by alembic in sand, and rectifying the spirit by cohobation three times until only the pure spirit rises. Cohobation was used specifically to concentrate the virtues of the material and to force successive purifications, each pass concentrating what the previous had prepared.

Applied to the shivambu distillate with plant material, cohobation has a clear logic: the first distillation prepares a clean, volatile-rich aqueous menstruum carrying the body's own signature compounds. Plant matter macerated in this prepared menstruum is being extracted into something already structured by biological processing. The second distillation then concentrates the combined volatile fractions — what the plant releases into what the body has already prepared — and separates them from the fixed material left in the pot.

---

## The Gap and What It Means

What does not appear clearly in the existing literature is a specific protocol that begins from distilled shivambu (rather than raw or aged urine), uses that as the primary menstruum for herbal maceration, and then redistills the strained infusion. The Damar Tantra works with fresh shivambu combined with plant preparations. The European tradition works with distilled spirit of urine as an alkahest primarily for mineral work, with plant work described in the broader Paracelsian literature using spirit of wine as the base.

The specific combination — distilled shivambu fraction as the first-pass menstruum for aromatic carminative herbs, followed by cohobation — is a natural synthesis of these two lineages that neither has documented in this exact form. This is not a gap that suggests the practice is without foundation. It is a gap that suggests the practice crosses a boundary — between the Indic tradition's understanding of shivambu as the active medium and the European tradition's technical elaboration of what distilled urine does as a solvent — that neither lineage was positioned to bridge from within its own framework.
