# The Vessel

*A retrospective account. Written from Sarandë, Albania — April 2026.*

---

I am writing this from a small apartment in Albania, looking out toward the Ionian Sea. We arrived here on the 14th of April — four days ago I was still in Spain. Before that, Montenegro. Before that, Canada. Before that, Latin America, and before that, Asia. Fourteen years of motion. My partner and I carry what we need. A laptop. A camera. A microphone. A countertop water distiller that cost less than a week's rent and has changed everything.

This is not the place for the theory. The theory is in the archive. This is the place where I tell you what actually happened — in a real body, in real time, in a rented apartment in Torremolinos, Spain, looking out over the Mediterranean that the priests of Isis called the spume of Typhon and refused to touch.

---

## Fourteen Years

We left Canada in 2012. Both of us. No permanent address, no fixed country, no plan beyond the next place. Mostly Asia in the early years — Thailand, Malaysia, Bali, Vietnam, China, back and forth. The kind of life that strips away what you thought you needed and leaves you with a clearer picture of what actually matters.

What actually matters is very simple. Health. Attention. The quality of what you put in your body and the quality of what your body produces from it. Everything else is furniture.

Four years ago, in Laos, we began the amaroli practice in earnest. Not as an experiment. As a commitment. Daily. The morning collection, the aging, the drinking, the observation. It changed the diet. The diet changed the practice. The body became quieter and more legible — I could read it more clearly, the way you can read a signal better when you turn down the background noise.

Two years in Asia. Then Latin America — two more years. The practice continuing. The body adapting, clarifying. Eight months in Canada last spring and summer, then Spain.

Spain was the destination for the distillation.

---

## February 18–19, 2026 — Torremolinos

Olivier went first.

He had aged his own plasma for the right period — catalogued as the first decan of Aquarius — and we ran the first distillation together on the evening of February 18th. Countertop distiller, a rental kitchen in Torremolinos, the Mediterranean outside the window.

The chamber was full. It foamed heavily at first — the volatile fraction breaking the surface before the distiller could contain it, creating a natural overflow that separated into two distinct stages before the main distillate came through. We collected everything. Labeled everything. Measured everything.

The Stage 1 overflow: 450–466 PPM. Active, alive, fluctuating. The material that comes over first — the lightest volatiles, the most penetrating fraction.

The Stage 2 distillate in the carafe: 18 PPM. Crystal clear. The kind of clarity that water almost never has. The background visible straight through it, as through air.

The chamber residue — what remained after we stopped — was dark amber-brown, dense, soft to the touch. We ran it through coffee filters. The texture was like body lotion. It smelled alive. The taste was deeply umami, organically salted — and this from a body that had been strictly sodium chloride-free for over a month. The body makes its own salts. The meter couldn't measure them: it maxed out at 9999 PPM and kept reading over the top.

This residue was actually why we were doing this at all.

For three years before the distiller arrived we had been reducing shivambu on the stove — open pan, slow heat — to concentrate it for topical use. We knew what reduced shivambu could do on the skin. We wanted more of it, and more reliably. When we ordered the distiller — from the airport, leaving Montenegro, so it would be waiting for us in Spain — we ordered it to make pure drinking water — we were tired of buying bottled water and a distiller was the obvious solution. It arrived in Spain and we set it up for exactly that purpose.

The idea to use it for shivambu came later. I suggested we take it outside — to avoid the smell in the apartment. When I mentioned it to Olivier, his eyes opened wide and he went still for a moment the way he does when something lands. *That's the best idea you've ever had.* He knew before I did that we were about to find something. Something in him recognised it before either of us could name it.

We took the distiller outside and ran the first reduction. And very quickly, before the run was even finished, the much larger realisation arrived: what was coming over in the collection carafe was something we had not anticipated at all. The water was not a byproduct. The water was its own substance entirely. Crystal clear. 18 PPM. The kind of clarity that changes what you think you're working with.

And then all of it fell into place at once.

Every alchemical text I had read over the years. The apparatus of Mary the Jewess. The language surrounding the prima materia — common, despised, golden, abundant, possessed by everyone. The dietary protocols of the priests of Isis. The Buddhist Vinaya. The Damar Tantra. All of it, held in pieces for years, suddenly assembling itself in a single moment of recognition.

I was holding something ancient. Touching it. Drinking it. Something older than the words used to describe it, older than the traditions that encoded it, older than the suppression that buried it. The cosmic waters. The prima materia that every tradition had pointed at and carefully not named.

I came apart the way you come apart when something real arrives. Like the love that moves through a mother the moment after birth — not chosen, not managed, just there. A puddle on the floor. The five thousand years of women standing behind me suddenly present, suddenly close, suddenly real in a way that no amount of reading had made them.

I took photographs. The crystal clear water in the jar held up to the light. And something happened even before it touched my lips — my mind became as clear as what I was holding. Not a thought about clarity. Clarity itself, arriving through the eyes.

Four years ago, when I first came into the amaroli practice, I felt the weight of what I was taking on. The rejection from the collective. The way I knew people would look at me. I remember feeling that I would be an advocate for this the way John Armstrong was — carrying it publicly, absorbing the resistance, making the case. I felt the burden of that before I had even fully begun.

This was different.

This was not advocacy. This was not a position to defend or a case to make. What I was holding was older than argument. It was older than the shame that had been placed around it, older than the institutions that had enforced that shame, older than the word that had been weaponised to make the question feel closed before it could be asked.

What I was holding was a lineage. And part of that lineage was in me — not metaphorically, not as inspiration, but as actual biological inheritance. The women of Alexandria who designed the apparatus. The women in every tradition who carried this knowledge in their bodies when the texts were burned. They were not behind me as symbols. They were present in the chemistry of what I was holding, in the same way that my mother is present in my body, in the same way that every woman who carried me forward is present whether I name her or not.

This is my dharma. Not a burden. Not a position. A fact about what I am and what I am here to do.

I took photographs. Not of the dark residue — I had seen that many times, reduced on the stove over three years, and my body knew it. What brought me to my knees was the clear water. The solar distillate in the carafe. Crystal clear, 18 PPM, the background visible straight through it as through air. I had never seen it before. No one had told me it existed. The dark I was familiar with. The clarity was new. The clarity was what broke something open.

And in that moment I did not care what the numbers were going to show. I did not need any validation from any meter. The energy moving through my body was the only reading that mattered — and it was not ambiguous.

## February 22 — The Golden Syphon

Before the vow, the preparation.

The batch I would distill that day had been incubating since February 1st — Sun in Aquarius, Moon in Leo. Twenty-one days of gestation. Before I ran it through the distiller I spent time with it in a way that I wouldn't necessarily call ritual but that isn't quite the right word to avoid.

I began internally. From the sister-bottle of this same Leo Full Moon batch — the same origin, the same frequency — tuning the internal vessel to the external one before the heat was applied. Microcosm and macrocosm locked into the same imprint. The body remembering February 1st so that the practitioner would be an undistorted conduit for what came next.

Then the solar incubation. Two days in direct sun to awaken the Tejas — the subtle fire — within the water. Kept warm through the night, returned to morning sun. Active, alive.

Then the syphon. The vessel elevated and left undisturbed, gravity doing the first alchemical separation — heavy sediment settling to the bottom, the clear essence rising. Under the seal of the yin-yang, only the uppermost fluid was syphoned off. Leaving the dense matter behind. Extracting only the clear mirror of the plasma.

And then the altar.

Beneath the vessel I placed what I think of as the ancestral battery: a handmade gold bracelet from Yellowknife, gifted at nineteen, carrying my father's lineage — pharmacist and goldsmith both, chemistry and noble metals in the same hands. A citrine teardrop in gold on silver. A 20-franc gold rooster coin. Memory made material. Lineage as anchor while the heat does its work.

Then distillation.

## February 22 — The Vow

My own first formal distillation came four days later. On that morning I wrote what became the founding document of this archive: a vow to observe without bias, to record without inflation, to treat the body as the laboratory and the record simultaneously.

*I vow to cultivate Shen-Ming. I will observe the holographic nature of these biological waters with the precision of a balanced scale, allowing my mind to reflect exactly what is, without the sediment of personal bias.*

This is still the operating principle. Everything in this archive is accountable to it.

## What the Practice Produces

I am not going to make a list of effects and call it a protocol. The archive has protocols. What I can offer here is more specific and more personal: what I have actually noticed in my own body, in the months since February, framed exactly as what it is — one practitioner's account, four years into the amaroli practice, three months into working with the distilled fractions.

The diet simplified. This is not discipline — it was not an effort. The desire for complexity fell away. The ambrosia diet, as I now call it, emerged naturally: well-cooked grains, good ghee, clean water. The Lunar fraction — the clear distillate — became my daily drinking water. I started feeling nourished and grounded at a level I don't have adequate language for. Not full. Not satisfied. Nourished. Grounded. The distinction matters.

The body became more sensitive to external chemical input. I could smell processed food at a distance and feel the response in my gum tissue — not an idea about it, a physical sensation. This sensitivity is information. I treat it as a reading of what the body actually tolerates rather than as a problem to be managed.

The dreaming in Spain was extraordinary. This is the dimension I am most careful about claiming, because it is the most subjective and the most easily inflated. What I will say plainly: the quality of sleep changed register. The dreaming became more coherent, more architecturally complex, more available to waking recall. I had a rebirthing experience in dream during this period — vivid, architectural, arriving with the force of something that needed to be recorded. That record is coming. It belongs to the record. I include it because it is honest — it happened and it seemed connected to the practice and I do not know more than that.

The eyes responded to the double-distilled Lunar fraction. I use it as eye drops, particularly after screen time and on days of travel. One episode — a severe migraine, the kind I have had rarely in the past — resolved through three applications over several hours. By the third application something shifted. I slept and woke free of it. I note this as a single documented episode. I do not know what it means beyond what it was.

The headaches have become rare since beginning the amaroli practice in general. I note this also. The distillation is new. The base practice is four years old. I cannot clearly separate their contributions.

## Corfu

We stopped in Corfu on the way here from Spain. I was not prepared for what that place would do to me.

The western coast of Greece, the Ionian Islands — this is the geography of the tradition I have been working to recover. The women of Alexandria, the routes of Hellenistic knowledge, the thread that connects Babylon to Egypt to Greece to Rome. Standing on that ground I felt the proximity of what I have been following in documents and historical fragments as a physical sensation. Close. The way you feel when you are near something real.

This could be projection. I know this. I hold it as observation rather than claim. But it happened, and the work is about honesty, so I am including it.

The grief piece in this archive is about the erasure of women's knowledge from the historical record. Corfu made that grief bodily for me. And then it was done. I crossed to Albania carrying something I hadn't carried before — a sense of direct lineage, not through texts, through place.

---

## Albania, April 2026

The practice continues here. The distiller runs. The archive grows. Others in the community are working with their own distillers now. Some have been with the raw practice longer than the distillation. The network is small and real and building.

The electric countertop distiller auto-shuts at approximately 100°C. This is — I was pleased to realise — exactly within the biological distillation range documented in the apparatus section of this archive. It is not a compromise. It is calibrated. It is also accessible: no glass laboratory equipment required, no specialist knowledge, no infrastructure. A rental kitchen and a countertop appliance. This was the condition I was working in and it produced what you see documented in the archive.

I have experience with glass distillation equipment — I have used it for herbal extracts in the past and I look forward to returning to it. The dream is a space south of Málaga, on the Spanish Mediterranean coast, where the climate is right and the geography is resonant and the practice can have what it needs: a laboratory, a kitchen, a garden, a place where people can come and learn and try the ambrosia diet and sit with the history of what this knowledge is.

I do not need to own that land. I want the project to outlive me. I only want to be part of bringing it into form and shaping it in a lasting way before I step back.

---

## Why I Am Doing This in Public

Because the alternative is that it disappears again.

I have been away from my family for most of these fourteen years. They do not fully understand the path I am on. I carry my tools — laptop, camera, microphone, distiller — and I travel light and I live simply and I work. This is the monastic form that corresponds to this practice. I did not choose it as aesthetics. It chose itself.

The women of Alexandria whose work I have spent months recovering left their knowledge in symbols because direct statement was not safe. I live in a different moment. Direct statement is possible, though it comes with its own forms of resistance — platform restrictions, automated systems that flag home laboratory work, the soft erasure of invisibility.

I am choosing to be as public as possible, precisely and specifically because I believe that what I have found is not mine to withhold. If someone with the means and the vision reads this and wants to help build the space in southern Spain — that is why I am writing it down. If someone in another part of the world reads this and begins their own practice and their own documentation — that is why I am writing it down.

The knowledge is in the body. Every human body. It cannot be permanently suppressed because it cannot be permanently separated from the people who carry it.

I am one person documenting one body's experience. I am also standing at the end of a very long line of women who documented theirs, in whatever form the moment permitted.

This archive is my form.
