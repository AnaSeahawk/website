# Claim Environments

Claim environments name the layer a sentence belongs to.

They do not rank the sentence by value. They clarify what kind of truth is being offered.

## Field

Direct experience. Something happened, was felt, was noticed, or was recorded in the living context of the practice.

Field language stays close to the event. It does not need to explain itself before it can be recorded.

## Instrument Table

Numerical or technical observation. This includes readings, conditions, dates, sample states, thresholds, instruments, and limitations.

Instrument language names what was measured and what was not measured.

For the archive's measurement shorthand, see the [Measurement Legend](measurement-legend.md).

## Pattern Room

Repeated observation. A single event has begun to appear across time, season, condition, or context.

Pattern language remains careful. Recurrence is meaningful, but recurrence is not proof of mechanism by itself.

## Interpretation Chamber

Meaning formed around observation. This is where the archive begins to ask what an event may signify.

Interpretive language should remain visible as interpretation.

## Hypothesis Forge

Provisional mechanism. A possible explanation is named so it can be questioned, refined, contradicted, or strengthened by future records.

Hypothesis language does not pretend to finality.

## Comparative Archive

Traditions, texts, symbols, histories, and parallel systems placed in relation.

Comparative language can show resonance and convergence. It does not automatically establish shared mechanism.

## Mythic Field

Image, story, descent, planet, vessel, rhythm, sacred narrative, and symbolic truth.

Mythic language may be true within human experience without being empirical evidence.

## Philosophical Ground

First principles openly declared.

Philosophical language names the commitments beneath the work, rather than hiding them inside scientific implication.

## Practice Ethic

The moral container of the work. This includes restraint, non-coercion, single-subject framing, non-medical language, and responsibility for transmission.

Practice-ethic language protects the archive from becoming directive, inflated, or extractive.
