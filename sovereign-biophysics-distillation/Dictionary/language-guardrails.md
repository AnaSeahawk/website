# Language Guardrails

The archive can hold many kinds of knowing at once.

The guardrails keep those kinds from impersonating one another.

## Non-Medical Boundary

This archive is not medical advice.

Do not frame an observation as treatment, cure, prevention, diagnosis, protocol for another body, or promised effect.

## Single-Subject Frame

The record belongs to one practitioner's body and context unless another source is explicitly named.

Use language that preserves locality: observed here, recorded in this run, in this body, under these conditions.

## Measurement Boundary

Measurements should name their conditions and limits.

A number is not a total explanation. It is one instrument's reading of one aspect of a sample at one time.

## Symbolic Boundary

Symbolic resonance is allowed to remain luminous.

It should not be used as empirical proof.

## Comparative Boundary

Parallels across traditions are meaningful, but resemblance is not identity.

Comparative language should distinguish echo, analogy, shared pattern, and documented continuity.

## Hypothesis Boundary

A hypothesis is an offering to future observation.

It should not be written as settled mechanism unless the evidence has actually reached that level.

## Operational Boundary

Terms used inside the practice may need translation when speaking to external scientific, historical, or public audiences.

When a word carries both internal and external meanings, name the active register.

## Restraint

Uncertainty is part of the record.

The archive remains stronger when it can say: observed, measured, interpreted, hypothesized, symbolic, unknown.
