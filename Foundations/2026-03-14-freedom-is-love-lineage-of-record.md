# Freedom is Love

**The Glimmering Pathways: The Visceral Archive and Legacy of Helen Humphreys Seargeant**
*[A Great-Niece's Retrospective]*

*Dedicated*
*to all Pioneers . . .*
*those adventurous souls*
*who led the way into wild, new lands*
*in search of the benefits*
*God might give them*
— Helen Humphreys Seargeant, *House by the Buckeye Road*

There is a visceral power in a well-kept record. A journal is not merely a reflection of the past; it is a mechanism to affect the future. It is a way of laying straight the truth when the passage of time threatens to bend it.

I take my own work—my archiving, my writing, and the architecture of the communities I build—so seriously precisely because of women like Helen Humphreys Seargeant. She is more than a historical influence on me; she is my great-aunt on my father's side. Her meticulous dedication to documenting the frontiers she survived is the foundational purpose behind why I run my own spaces the way I do today. It is why our community calls are systematically recorded, why information is fiercely preserved, and why the identities of those who share their truths are kept securely safe when requested. We are building a living archive of our own time, honoring the exact same impulse that drove my great-aunt to put pen to paper a century ago.

Her life was defined by a rare geographical and spiritual symmetry, stretching from the humid, volcanic slopes of southern Mexico to the sun-bleached plains of the American Southwest. A pioneer, a diarist, and a historian of the unwritten, Helen anchored these shifting frontiers in the written word, ensuring that the glimmering pathways of our family and those who went before were never truly lost.

### **The Jungle Roots: San Antonio Nexapa**

Born in 1878 in Prescott, Arizona, Helen’s life took a dramatic turn when her parents sought a new destiny in the lush Soconusco region of Chiapas, Mexico. At the foot of the Tacaná volcano, the family carved a plantation—*San Antonio Nexapa*—out of the raw, dripping jungle.

This era, immortalized in her 1952 memoir of the same name, captures the grueling labor of the Porfiriato period (1876–1911). Helen detailed a life where homes were built with broad-axed wooden beams, with roofs erected before the walls just to survive the relentless tropical rains.

The terrifying vulnerability of this existence is captured viscerally in Chapter 23 of the book. The year 1902 brought massive volcanic activity to the region. Ash and seismic upheaval blanketed the landscape, which made gardening nearly impossible and severely impacted the coffee harvest for the year. Notably, this catastrophic geological event—recognized in chronological data as the 1902 Phoenix Event—aligns precisely with the 138-year cycle of historical upheaval confirmed by Jason Breshears. It was a chapter of sheer survival. The family only escaped financial ruin because they had the foresight to cultivate a sugar plantation alongside the coffee. It is a stark, honest record of the hard truths of frontier life: survival often hinged on a single crop, a shift in the wind, or the sudden eruption of the earth itself.

### **An Academic Legacy in Chiapas**

Because of the precision of her records, Helen’s work is not regarded merely as a personal memoir in Mexico, but as a vital primary historical source. Her vivid descriptions of 19th-century *finca* life are preserved in the most prestigious cultural corners of Chiapas, most notably within the library of Casa Na Bolom in San Cristóbal de las Casas. This 18th-century monastery, turned heritage sanctuary by Frans and Gertrude Blom, protects the exact kind of regional history my great-aunt wrote about.

Recognizing its immense ongoing value to anthropologists and historians studying the agricultural boom of the Soconusco region, the state government and the Universidad de Ciencias y Artes de Chiapas (UNICACH) translated and republished her work in Spanish as part of the esteemed *Colección Ceiba*. This academic recognition ensures her meticulously kept records continue to serve as an active, studied part of the region's history today.

### **Severing the Ties: The Departure from Chiapas**

The brutal realities of the 1902 earthquakes and eruptions marked a turning point. Sometime after surviving that harrowing year, Helen made the bold decision to leave the plantation on her own, venturing out independently before the rest of the family finalized the sale of San Antonio Nexapa in 1905.

When her parents finally sold the estate, they made a radical, defining choice for the era. During the Porfiriato, agricultural workers were traditionally tied to the plantations through a system of "debt peonage"—inheriting insurmountable debts owed to the company store that bound them to the land. Upon selling, our family systematically forgave these debts, legally releasing the workers from indentured servitude. It was a profound act of severance and liberation, closing their chapter in Chiapas by granting the exact freedom Helen would later write about.

### **The Return West and the Canyon Mythbuster**

Around the turn of the century, her family relocated to Riverside, California, where Helen attended business school. This formal education undoubtedly sharpened the meticulous, administrative discipline she would later rely on to maintain decades of detailed journals. She eventually returned to the rugged frontier of her birth, moving to Phoenix, Arizona, around 1911.

Re-engaging with the American Southwest meant encountering her family's formidable pioneer legacy. It is here that the true power of the archive reveals itself.

In the August 1959 issue of *Arizona Highways*, Helen published an article titled "Mooney Falls," which dismantled a long-standing piece of Grand Canyon lore. Popular legend claimed a miner named James Mooney hung for days on a jammed rope before falling to his death. Using her uncle Alphonso’s preserved private letters, Helen laid straight the truth: it was her own father, Matthew Humphreys—my great-grandfather—who truly braved the heights.

Nearly a year after Mooney’s fatal fall, Matthew was the man who, dangling precariously from a rope, dynamited the sheer travertine cliff face and drilled the iron spikes into the rock. Those same spikes and tunnels remain the only way for modern hikers to descend the 196-foot falls today. My great-grandfather built the physical path; my great-aunt, through her archive, built the historical one.

### **The Desert Spirit: The Buckeye Road**

In 1913, Helen married Jonathan William Seargeant and began a second act that was the polar opposite of her tropical youth. Settling on a 160-acre ranch near Cashion, Arizona, she transitioned from the damp shade of coffee trees to the harsh, unforgiving grit of desert farming.

This period was documented in her 1960 book, *House by the Buckeye Road*. But Helen did not merely survive the desert; she actively shaped its cultural and historical fabric. During these decades, she became a recognized pillar of the community, cementing her dedication to her craft as an active member of the Phoenix Branch of the National League of American Pen Women. Her deep pioneer roots—having been born in Prescott in 1878, long before Arizona statehood—earned her a rightful place in the exclusive First Families of Arizona. And, perhaps as a quiet nod to her father’s and uncle's rugged legacy at the Grand Canyon, she was also a dedicated member of the local Mineralogical Society.

Today, the Arizona Historical Society in Tempe serves as the steward of this American legacy. Within their archives lie seventeen of her personal, handwritten diaries spanning from the 1930s through the 1960s. These fragile journals capture the granular details of Maricopa County’s development, proving that no daily truth is too small to be recorded, and no history is too distant to be preserved.

### **FOLLOW ON**

*By Helen Humphreys Seargeant*

There is many a one who has sailed away
In Adventure's ship to a brighter day,
Like heroes of old, their standards high,
And found their goals, and why not I?

They searched for a land where souls are free—
And left a trail that is plain to me—
For they set their sails in the pearly dawn,
And their glimmering pathways followed on.

Their cargo was one we all may take,
And I who never the spell would break,
Will bind mine well in its joints and seams—
That precious coffer of golden dreams.

I'll guard it well in the stoutest hold,
And sail again, like the men of old,
To prove that dreams are the power of youth,
And to find the place where dreams are truth.

The wind blows sweet. It's the break of day!
My ship runs free with the flying spray,
The sails gleam white on my own bright morn—
Let the glimmering pathways follow on.
